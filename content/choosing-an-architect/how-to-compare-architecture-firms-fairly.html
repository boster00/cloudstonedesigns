Section 01

Why Comparing Firms Fairly Is Harder Than It Looks

Most evaluation processes inadvertently favor the firm that communicates best, not the firm that will perform best.

When you ask three or four design firms for proposals, you are not receiving comparable documents. Each firm has a house format shaped by what it has learned to emphasize. Some bury hourly rates inside appendices. Some conflate phases with deliverables. Some describe process at length while providing no evidence it is actually followed.

The result: you spend hours reading materials that cannot be compared directly, and decision anxiety compounds. The firm with the most polished proposal often wins—regardless of fit.

The core problem

A comparison process that tolerates incompatible inputs will produce a decision based on presentation quality, not project suitability. Your job is to impose a common structure before you receive proposals, not after.

This guide gives you a reproducible framework: what to ask for in a standardized way, how to interpret what you receive, what omissions mean, and which questions consistently reveal the real differences between firms.

Before You Request Proposals

  • Write a one-page project brief all firms receive identically
  • Define evaluation criteria before reading any proposals
  • Set a common deadline (fairness and professionalism signal)
  • Specify exactly which sections you need in every response
  • Ask for at least two references per firm upfront, not later
  • Identify your internal decision-maker before the process begins
Section 02

How to Read a Proposal—and What to Demand in One

A proposal is not a marketing document. It is a testable claim about how work will be structured, staffed, and priced.

Most design firm proposals mix three very different things: capability marketing, project methodology, and financial terms. These deserve separate attention. When they are blended, it is almost always intentional—a stronger marketing section compensates for a weaker scope or fee section.

When reviewing proposals, separate the document into three buckets and evaluate each independently before forming an overall impression.

Proposal Evaluation Grid
Section What to look for Red flag Green flag
Scope definition Numbered deliverables with acceptance criteria Vague "Brand identity system" Specific "Primary logo in 3 formats + usage guide"
Phase breakdown Named phases, durations, and your review points None No phases listed Clear Discovery / Concept / Refine / Deliver
Staffing Named or role-named individuals and their time allocation Generic "Senior designer assigned" Named Specific person, confirmed availability
Revision policy Number of rounds included per deliverable Missing No revision language at all Explicit "2 rounds of revisions per phase"
Change order process How out-of-scope work is estimated and authorized Absent No mention of scope changes Defined Written CO required before work begins
Payment schedule Tied to phases or deliverables, not calendar dates Calendar Monthly invoices regardless of progress Milestone Payment linked to accepted deliverables
Watch for this pattern

A proposal that is long on portfolio and short on scope is telling you something. Firms that are confident in their process lead with structure. Firms that are less confident lead with aesthetic appeal.

Mandatory Proposal Sections

  • Numbered deliverable list
  • Phase timeline with client review windows
  • Named team members, not just titles
  • Revision rounds per phase, in writing
  • IP transfer terms and timing
  • Total all-in fee (not starting from)
  • Change order policy
  • Primary point of contact for duration of engagement
Section 03

Fee Structure Transparency: What the Number Actually Means

A quoted fee is not a total cost. Understanding the pricing model is more important than comparing headline numbers.

Design firms use several pricing models, each with different risk profiles for the client. Before comparing fees across firms, identify which model each firm is using—comparing a fixed-fee proposal to an hourly estimate is not a meaningful comparison.

Fixed Fee
Lowest client risk if scope is tight. Works well when deliverables are well-defined upfront. Watch for artificially narrow scope definitions that generate change orders. Ask: what triggers a change order, and at what hourly rate?
Hourly / Time & Materials
High variance, maximum flexibility. Useful for exploratory or iterative work. Requires you to define a budget cap and approval thresholds explicitly. Ask: what is the billing increment, and who authorizes overages?
Retainer / Phase-gated
Common for ongoing or multi-phase work. Often favorable for firms when scope is loosely defined. Ask: what deliverables are guaranteed per period, and how is unused time handled?

Beyond the pricing model, request a detailed breakdown of where the fee is allocated by phase and role. This reveals two things: whether the firm is front-loading discovery (a positive sign of process maturity) and whether the quoted rate reflects the seniority of the team that will actually do the work.

The bait-and-switch pattern

A senior principal presents the proposal and leads early meetings. After the contract is signed, day-to-day work is executed by junior staff at the same rate. Ask directly: who will be working on this engagement week to week, and what is their billing rate?

Visual module: Fee allocation breakdown chart (phase-by-phase, per firm)

Three Fee Questions That Reveal the Most

  1. What is your effective hourly rate for this project, all-in? Forces transparency even in fixed-fee structures.
  2. What has caused the largest cost overruns in past comparable projects, and by how much? Tests honesty about risk and their own track record.
  3. If we need to stop at the end of Phase 1, what would we owe and what would we own? Reveals how payment and IP transfer are actually structured.

Fee Transparency Checklist

  • Identify pricing model (fixed / T&M / retainer)
  • Confirm total all-in cost, not a starting-from price
  • Get phase-level fee allocation in writing
  • Confirm change order rate and approval process
  • Clarify what triggers a change order vs. included revisions
  • Confirm which team members bill at which rate
  • Ask about historical overrun rate on comparable work
Section 04

Process Documentation: Described vs. Evidenced

Every firm has a process deck. The question is whether there is evidence it is actually used—and whether it is appropriate for your project type.

Process slides are marketing. The indicators of a real, practiced methodology are different: they show up in proposals, contracts, and the answers to specific questions. Below is what to look for.

1
Discovery Stakeholder interviews, audit, brief
2
Strategy Positioning, criteria for good work
3
Concept Options presented with rationale
4
Development Refinement against approved direction
5
Delivery Files, documentation, handoff

How to test whether a firm actually uses its stated process

Ask for a sample project brief or kickoff document from a completed project (anonymized is fine). Ask for a sample creative brief or direction-alignment document from Phase 2. If they cannot produce these, the process exists on slides only.

A firm that uses a genuine discovery process will be able to articulate, in the proposal, the specific questions they need to answer before design begins—and how they plan to answer them. Vague answers to "what does your discovery phase look like?" are a reliable signal.

The single best process question

Ask: "Walk me through the last time a client rejected your initial concepts. What happened next?" A firm with a real process will answer this with specifics. A firm without one will pivot to talking about how well clients typically respond to their work.

What to look for in process documentation

  • Does the proposal name specific discovery activities (interviews, audits, competitive analysis)?
  • Is there a defined point at which strategy is locked before design begins?
  • Are the criteria for evaluating design options stated in the proposal (not just aesthetic preference)?
  • Is there a documented handoff procedure, or does delivery just mean "files sent"?
  • Are client review periods built into the timeline, with defined turnaround expectations from both sides?

Process Evidence to Request

  • Sample kickoff questionnaire or brief template
  • Sample creative brief or direction alignment doc
  • Sample project status report format
  • Sample deliverable or brand guidelines document
  • Example of a change order or scope addendum
Section 05

What Firms Show vs. What They Hide

The most important information in a vendor comparison is rarely in the proposal. It is in what is absent, avoided, or only visible under direct questioning.

Transparency Signals
  • Acknowledges where this project type is not their strongest
  • Proactively discloses current workload and availability
  • Names who specifically will execute the work
  • Provides unsolicited references from difficult or challenging projects
  • Discusses how they handle disagreements about creative direction
  • Offers to show work-in-progress files, not just final deliverables
  • States revision limits clearly without being asked
Concealment Signals
  • Pivots every process question back to portfolio examples
  • Cannot name the person who will lead day-to-day work
  • References are all from one sector or time period
  • Fee language is ambiguous about what triggers additional charges
  • Cannot explain what happens if you are unhappy at concept stage
  • Resists showing sample deliverables or process artifacts
  • Timeline listed as "TBD" or "to be confirmed at kickoff"

The omission test

After reading each proposal, note what is not addressed. Firms experienced with complex or contested projects typically address scope ambiguity, change management, and creative disagreement upfront—because they have been burned before. A proposal that assumes everything will go smoothly is a proposal written by a firm that has not done enough projects to know otherwise, or one that is deliberately avoiding the conversation.

What timeline vagueness actually means

When a proposal says "timeline to be confirmed at kickoff," it typically means one of three things: the firm is currently overcommitted and cannot guarantee start dates, their scheduling process is reactive rather than planned, or they are reserving flexibility to deprioritize your project if a larger one comes in. Ask them directly which it is.

Section 06

Questions That Produce the Most Useful Differentiating Answers

Standard interview questions produce rehearsed answers. These questions are designed to surface information firms do not prepare for.

  • 01 Tell me about a project where the client did not like your initial creative direction. What did you do, and what was the outcome?

    Why it works

    This question tests both process maturity and client relationship management. Strong firms will answer with a specific example, explain how they separated the strategic question from the aesthetic one, and describe what they learned. Firms without a real process will give a vague answer or claim it "rarely happens." It always happens.

  • 02 What information would you need before you could tell us whether our budget is realistic for what we are asking for?

    Why it works

    A capable firm will answer with a specific list of scope questions. A firm that confirms your budget is workable without asking a single clarifying question is either inexperienced or agreeing to anything to win the contract. Neither is what you want.

  • 03 What is the most common reason projects in your experience go over budget or schedule, and what specifically do you do to prevent it?

    Why it works

    Firms with operational experience will answer with candid, specific observations (scope ambiguity, client feedback latency, approval chain delays). Firms without it will offer generic reassurances. The best answer is also self-aware: what the firm does to cause delays, not just what clients do.

  • 04 Who will actually be doing the work on this project week to week, and can I meet them before we sign?

    Why it works

    This is the most direct test for the bait-and-switch staffing pattern. A firm that resists this question, or that cannot name the person, is signaling a problem. Any firm confident in its team will be glad to make the introduction. Note that some smaller firms may not have confirmed resourcing until a project is closer to starting—but they should be able to say exactly when and how that decision is made.

  • 05 What would cause you to recommend we do not proceed with this project as scoped, or recommend a different approach entirely?

    Why it works

    This question tests whether the firm is capable of advising against its own commercial interest. The best firms will give a thoughtful, direct answer. It also opens a productive conversation about scope and approach that often surfaces important considerations neither party had surfaced. A firm that cannot conceive of a scenario where they would push back is not the advisor relationship you want.

  • 06 How do you handle it when a key stakeholder on our side gives feedback that contradicts an earlier approved direction?

    Why it works

    This tests for scope and change management maturity. Firms with good process will describe a written direction-approval mechanism that protects both parties. Firms without it will say "we work it out" or "we are flexible"—which in practice means rework without compensation, which means eventual resentment and corners cut.

Section 07

Reference Checks: What to Ask and Who to Find

A firm-provided reference list is a curated sample. It is useful, but it is not neutral. Here is how to get more signal from it—and how to go beyond it.

Every firm provides their best references. That is expected and appropriate. The way to get useful information from provided references is not to ask different people—it is to ask better questions. The questions below are designed to produce usable information even from references who are positively disposed toward the firm.

In parallel, use the firm's published work and case studies to find clients they have not offered as references. A brief, direct LinkedIn or email inquiry to someone listed in a case study will often produce candid, unprepared answers that are more informative than any curated reference call.

What was the hardest part of working with this firm?

Every engagement has friction. A reference who claims there was none is either not being candid or had an unusually simple project. The answer tells you what type of problem to anticipate.

Did the project come in at the quoted fee? If not, by how much and why?

One of the most directly informative questions. Scope creep, change orders, and billing surprises are common enough that most references will have a real answer.

Who did the actual work, and how often did you interact with them versus the person who sold you the project?

Tests for the staffing bait-and-switch. Also tells you about communication patterns and accessibility.

Knowing what you know now, what would you do differently in how you managed the engagement?

A slightly indirect question that produces candid answers about where the process was weak—both from the firm and the client side.

Did the deliverables perform as expected after launch or implementation?

Moves beyond the production quality of the work to its actual effectiveness. Rare and valuable. Most design evaluations focus only on the former.

Would you hire them again, and if yes, for what type of project specifically?

The qualifier "for what type of project" is the important part. Enthusiasm without specificity is less useful than a qualified endorsement scoped to a particular kind of work.

Finding references beyond the provided list

Review the firm's case studies and portfolio. For any project listed, the client company is identifiable. A brief LinkedIn message to someone in a relevant role at that company—"I'm evaluating this firm for a project and saw your name associated with their work—would you be willing to share a few thoughts?"—will produce a response rate of roughly 30–40%, and those conversations are consistently the most informative of any in the process.

Reference Call Protocol

  • Call, do not email (people speak more candidly than they write)
  • Keep it to 15–20 minutes with 4–6 prepared questions
  • Ask about specific project type, not general impressions
  • Ask for at least one area where they fell short
  • Note hesitations as carefully as endorsements
  • Seek at least one reference not provided by the firm
Section 08

Evaluating Relevant Experience: Proximity to Your Problem

Industry experience and project-type experience are different things. Understanding which one matters more for your situation will change how you weight portfolios.

Most buyers evaluate portfolio work on two axes: quality and sector familiarity. Both matter, but neither is as important as problem-type familiarity: has this firm solved the specific challenge you are facing, regardless of sector?

A firm that has built multiple brand identities for regulated industries may be more relevant to your pharmaceutical rebrand than a firm with deep healthcare sector experience that has only done digital product design. Map your actual design challenge, then look for evidence of that problem type in the portfolio—not just the industry label.

Experience Relevance Framework
Experience Type What it signals Limitations Weight it if…
Sector / Industry Familiarity with regulatory, audience, and competitive context Does not confirm design problem-type skill Regulatory or compliance constraints are high
Project type (e.g. rebrands) Practiced methodology for your specific challenge May lack sector-specific knowledge Process complexity and stakeholder management are the primary risks
Organization size / complexity Ability to manage multiple stakeholders, approval chains Smaller clients may not have tested at your scale Internal alignment and organizational buy-in are the primary challenge
Recency of similar work Current methodologies and active practice Past experience may be stale or not reflective of current team Technology, platform, or format context evolves quickly

Portfolio review questions to ask each firm

  • For each relevant case study: who was the day-to-day lead, and are they still at the firm?
  • What was the most difficult aspect of that project, and how did you resolve it?
  • How much of that project was strategic and how much was execution? (Tells you their actual role.)
  • What would you do differently on that project now?
  • Is that client available as a reference?

Portfolio Review Checklist

  • Confirm lead designer on each relevant case study is still at the firm
  • Identify problem type (not just sector) in each example
  • Note organization size and internal complexity of past clients
  • Check recency: is relevant work within the last 3 years?
  • Ask about actual role vs. contribution (strategy, execution, or both)
  • Request case study on a project that did not go as planned
