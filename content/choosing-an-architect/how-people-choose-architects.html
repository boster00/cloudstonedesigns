01 How the search usually starts

The four ways most people find an architect

Most people have never hired an architect before. When it comes time to start, they default to a small number of reliable-feeling methods. Each has merit, but each also has a blind spot worth knowing about.

Diagram — four search channel breakdown
  • 1

    Referrals from friends, family, or contractors

    The most common starting point and often the best. A referral carries built-in social accountability that no portfolio can replicate. The limitation: the person referring you may have had a different budget, a different brief, or a different tolerance for friction. Their ideal architect may not be yours.

  • 2

    Portfolio and awards browsing

    Architecture publications, Instagram, Pinterest, and awards shortlists all create a sense of visual shortlisting. The trap is obvious in hindsight: the most photographed projects are rarely the most liveable. Photographers are hired to make buildings look extraordinary. They succeed.

  • 3

    Online search and directories

    Google Maps, Houzz, and professional association directories are increasingly the first stop for those without a warm referral. They are useful for geographic filtering but offer limited signal on the qualities that actually matter to a client mid-project.

  • 4

    Walking past a building they admired

    Surprisingly common. A homeowner notices a house under construction or recently completed and asks the owners who designed it. This is an underrated signal: you already know the output looks right in context. The problem is that finding the architect this way is hit-and-miss.

Worth noting: None of these methods is wrong. Most successful projects start with one of them. The issue is what comes next — specifically, which questions people ask once they have a shortlist.
02 Decision criteria

What most people use to decide — and what actually predicts success

Most people narrow their shortlist using a handful of criteria. Some of those criteria are genuinely predictive. Others feel important but are largely noise.

Criterion How commonly used Predictive of success?
Visual style of portfolio Very commonly used as primary filter Weak. Style changes; working relationship does not.
Awards and recognition Frequently cited as a trust signal Weak. Awarded projects prioritise peers over clients.
Fee proposal / lowest cost Often used as a tiebreaker Inversely correlated. Low fees often mean less involvement.
Practice size and office location Moderate use as a proxy for capability Neutral. Larger does not mean more attention to your project.
Specific experience with your project type Considered but often deprioritised Strong. Fewer surprises in planning, construction, and cost.
Communication style in initial meetings Underweighted; treated as informal impression Very strong. Projects run 12–36 months. Communication matters enormously.
References from recent clients with similar projects Rarely requested; often skipped Very strong. The single most underused screening tool.
Clarity on fee scope and what is and is not included Often glossed over until a dispute arises Strong. Fee ambiguity is one of the top causes of client-architect breakdown.
"The best architects for your project are rarely the most famous. They are the ones who have solved problems like yours, who communicate in a way that makes you feel heard, and who are honest about what they do not know." — Cloudstone Designs
03 Learning from others

The most common regrets in architect selection

After speaking with hundreds of homeowners at various stages of their projects, a consistent set of themes emerges. These are not complaints about architecture itself — most people are genuinely happy with their finished buildings. They are regrets about the selection and engagement process.

Chart — anonymised client feedback data
“

I did not check references

Almost universally, people who experienced serious mid-project friction say they skipped reference checks. It felt awkward to ask, or they assumed the portfolio spoke for itself. It does not.

“

I chose on aesthetic alone

Falling in love with a portfolio is easy. Several homeowners describe choosing architects whose visual work they admired but whose process — responsiveness, documentation, site presence — did not match their expectations.

“

The fee structure was unclear from the start

Ambiguity about what is included in the fee — consultant coordination, contract administration, variation assessment — is one of the most common sources of friction. Many clients only discovered the gaps when they needed those services.

“

The principal I met was not the person who worked on my project

In larger practices, the partner who pitches the job may not be the architect who designs it. Clarifying who will actually lead the work — and be present on site — is a question worth asking explicitly.

“

I did not understand the planning risk

Some projects face genuine planning difficulty. Clients who were not briefed on those risks early found themselves surprised by delays, extra costs, and the need for redesign. That conversation should happen at the very first meeting.

“

I rushed the decision

External pressure — a land settlement deadline, a builder’s availability window — led several people to appoint an architect before completing proper due diligence. Genuine urgency is rare. Most timelines have more flex than they feel like they do.

04 A better approach

A better framework for evaluating architects

The goal is not to find the most acclaimed architect. It is to find the right architect for your project, your budget, and your working style. This framework shifts focus from surface signals to the things that actually correlate with good experiences.

Diagram — four-pillar evaluation framework

1. Relevant experience with your project type

Not just “residential” in general. Ask how many projects of your scale, on sites like yours, in your local planning jurisdiction, they have completed in the past three years. Specificity here pays off later.

2. Communication fit

How quickly do they respond to your initial enquiry? Do they listen more than they talk in the first meeting? Do you understand everything they say, or does the technical language feel designed to impress rather than inform? These patterns compound over two years.

3. Fee transparency

Request a written scope of services alongside the fee. A good architect can tell you exactly what is and is not included, what triggers additional fees, and how they handle variations. Vagueness here is a signal, not an accident.

4. Process clarity

Can they walk you through their typical design process, stage by stage? Do they have a clear view of the approvals pathway for your site? Can they name two or three things that might go wrong and how they would handle them? Honest answers here, including “I don’t know but here is how I would find out,” are a positive sign.

5. References from comparable clients

Ask for two or three clients whose projects were similar in scope to yours. Call them. Ask whether the project came in near the original cost estimate, whether the architect was easy to reach, and whether they would use this architect again. A five-minute call is worth more than ten hours of portfolio browsing.

A practical note: Most architects will respect you more, not less, for asking careful questions. Those who seem put off by due diligence are, themselves, a signal worth heeding.
05 Due diligence

Questions worth asking in an initial meeting

You do not need to interrogate anyone. Most of these arise naturally in a good conversation. The ones that do not come up naturally are often the most revealing.

About their experience

  • — How many projects of this type have you completed in the last three years?
  • — Who will be my primary contact throughout the project?
  • — Can you describe a project where the budget was challenged and how you handled it?

About the fee and scope

  • — What is included in the base fee, and what typically falls outside it?
  • — How do you handle variations once construction has started?
  • — Will you be present on site during construction, and how often?

About the process

  • — Walk me through your typical design process from brief to building permit.
  • — What are the most common planning issues on sites like mine?
  • — What would make this project harder than average, and how would you handle it?

About their clients

  • — Can I speak with two or three past clients whose projects were similar to mine?
  • — What do your clients tend to find most challenging about working with you?
  • — Is there a type of client or project you are not well-suited to?
