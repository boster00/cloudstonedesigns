Why Timelines Vary So Much

The single most honest answer is: scope and jurisdiction. A 2,500 sf residential addition in a straightforward suburb moves on a different clock than a 12,000 sf mixed-use commercial project in a jurisdiction with design review boards, environmental overlays, and union-labor bidding requirements.

There is no universal schedule. What an architect can give you is a phase-by-phase estimate calibrated to your project type, size, and location — and a frank account of where the real variables live.

The sections below break down each major phase, give you honest duration ranges, and flag the decisions and external factors that most commonly push a date to the right.

The AIA divides architectural services into five design phases, followed by permitting and construction administration. Each phase has a different pace depending on whether you are doing a residential renovation, a new custom home, or a commercial / mixed-use project.

The table below gives realistic ranges. "Best case" assumes fast, decisive client feedback, no scope changes, and a cooperative jurisdiction. "Typical" is what experienced architects actually plan to.

Pre-Design & Programming 2 – 4 wks 3 – 6 wks 4 – 10 wks Site analysis, surveys, code research, client goal alignment. Often underestimated.
Schematic Design (SD) 3 – 6 wks 4 – 8 wks 6 – 14 wks Conceptual plans, massing, key decisions. Major scope lock should happen here.
Design Development (DD) 4 – 7 wks 5 – 10 wks 8 – 16 wks Materials, systems, structural coordination. Changes here start to cost real money.

What Causes Slippage — and How to Mitigate It

Most schedule overruns are predictable. They cluster around a handful of patterns we see repeatedly. Each one is avoidable — or at least manageable — when you know it is coming.

  1. Scope creep during Schematic Design

    Every program addition after SD approval cascades into DD and CD rework. A second master bedroom added in DD typically costs 3–5 weeks and design fees for revision. The mitigation is a formal scope-lock sign-off before DD begins.

    Mitigation: formal SD sign-off

  2. Slow client decisions on finish selections

    Tile, cabinet, appliance, and fixture specifications are on the critical path for CDs. A three-week delay in confirming kitchen cabinetry holds the entire document set. Allocate dedicated selection time in the project calendar.

    Mitigation: selection schedule agreed at project kickoff

  3. Permit correction cycles

    Plan check comments are routine, but each correction round in most jurisdictions adds 2–6 weeks. Thorough, code-compliant CDs minimize rounds, but cannot eliminate them. Budget at least one correction cycle in your schedule.

    Mitigation: pre-application meeting with building dept.

  4. Contractor availability gaps

    Bidding in a hot market can add 4–8 weeks if your preferred contractors are booked and multiple re-bid rounds are needed. Starting contractor outreach during late CDs — before permit issuance — compresses this gap.

    Mitigation: early contractor outreach during CDs

  5. Long-lead material procurement

    Custom windows, structural steel, elevator cabs, and imported stone can carry 16– to 30-week lead times. Identifying these items in SD and issuing purchase orders before permit can recover months of schedule.

    Mitigation: early procurement list from GC at start of CDs

  6. Slow RFI and submittal response during construction

    The construction schedule depends on 24–48 hour RFI turnarounds. Delays in architect or client responses stall trades on site. A defined response-time protocol in the owner-architect agreement prevents this.

    Mitigation: contractual RFI response window

Architect-Controlled vs. Contractor-Controlled Time

One of the most practical distinctions clients miss: your architect controls the design schedule; your contractor controls the construction schedule. These are governed by different contracts, different professionals, and different risk factors. Understanding the line between them lets you hold the right party accountable.

Architect controls

  • Pre-design and programming duration
  • Schematic Design pace and iteration cycles
  • Design Development coordination with engineers
  • Construction Document completion and quality
  • Permit application submission timing
  • RFI and submittal response turnaround
  • Change order processing speed

Contractor controls

  • Subcontractor scheduling and sequencing
  • Material ordering and lead-time management
  • Labor crew deployment on site
  • Site safety and inspection readiness
  • Weather delay recovery plans
  • Punch list completion pace
  • Certificate of occupancy coordination
The grey zone: Permitting is neither. The jurisdiction sets the pace. An architect can improve the odds with thorough documents and a pre-application meeting; a contractor can accelerate inspections with good site management. But neither can override a four-month plan check queue. Build this into your date assumptions, not your mitigation list.
05

What “Fast-Track” Actually Costs

Fast-tracking — overlapping design and construction phases rather than sequencing them — is real and sometimes appropriate. But it comes with specific, knowable costs. It is not a free acceleration; it is a trade of money and risk for time.

The three mechanisms for compressing a schedule each carry a different price structure:

Mechanism 01
Design-Build Overlap

Starting early site work or foundation before full CDs are complete. Saves 8–14 weeks on paper. Costs: construction change orders increase 15–25% because field conditions are resolved with live crews rather than on drawings. Risk is highest if structural or MEP surprises emerge after foundations are poured.

Mechanism 02
Expedited Plan Check

Most jurisdictions offer over-the-counter or paid-accelerated review for an additional fee. Cost ranges from $500 to $15,000+ depending on project size and municipality. Cuts permitting time by 30–60%. Only available for straightforward projects without discretionary review.

Mechanism 03
Extended Hours & Fees

Architects can compress document production with additional staff and overtime. Expect a 20–35% fee premium for genuinely compressed schedules. The design quality risk is real: rushed documents generate more RFIs and change orders during construction, often costing more than the acceleration saved.

The honest calculation: Fast-tracking typically makes financial sense when a funding window closes, a lease obligation has a hard date, or a seasonal construction window would otherwise be missed. It rarely makes sense as a default. The best schedule compression tool is decisive client feedback — it costs nothing and protects quality.

Planning Around a Hard Date

If you have a fixed occupancy target — a business opening date, a school enrollment deadline, a lease expiry, or a funding milestone — start at that date and work backwards. This is called a reverse schedule, and it is the most useful planning tool we can offer.

Three questions define the planning exercise:

Questions to answer first

  • What is the hard date, and is it truly immovable?
  • What project type and size are we working with?
  • What is the permitting jurisdiction, and what is current review time?
  • Is this a renovation of occupied space or new construction?
  • Are there design review boards, HOA approvals, or other discretionary steps?

What the reverse schedule reveals

  • Whether the start date is already missed
  • Which phases must be compressed and at what premium
  • Where float exists vs. where the schedule is truly critical-path
  • Which long-lead items need early procurement commitments
  • What the latest possible decision date is for scope lock
Visual: Reverse schedule planning diagram

If the reverse schedule shows a start date that has passed, a frank conversation about scope reduction, phased construction, or date adjustment is worth having before the first drawing is made. An honest schedule at the outset protects the relationship, the budget, and the building.

How We Manage Your Schedule

We build a project-specific schedule at kickoff, review it with you at each phase transition, and flag slippage risks before they become problems. A few specific practices matter:

At kickoff

  • We research current permit review times for your jurisdiction before projecting a schedule
  • We identify long-lead items in the project type and flag early procurement needs
  • We build contingency float into phases we do not control
  • We agree on a decision calendar for major selections

Throughout design

  • Phase transition sign-offs lock scope and prevent downstream rework
  • Weekly status notes document open decisions and due dates
  • We flag permit-correction risk items before submission
  • We communicate RFI turnaround expectations to the contractor at the construction kickoff
