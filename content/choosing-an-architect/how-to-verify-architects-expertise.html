Verifying Licensure: What It Proves and What It Does Not

Every state maintains a public database of licensed architects. Verifying that the person you are speaking with holds an active license in the state where your project will be built is the single non-negotiable step in any evaluation. Without it, construction documents cannot legally be stamped, and permitted work cannot proceed.

Most state licensing boards publish their registries online. Search for the architect's full name exactly as it appears on their business materials. Confirm the license is active, not expired or suspended, and that it covers the state where your project will be permitted.

What a license confirms

A valid license tells you the architect passed a multi-part national exam, completed a supervised internship period, and has not had their license revoked for disciplinary reasons. It does not tell you whether they are experienced in your project type, whether they will manage your budget effectively, or whether they communicate well.

Larger firms often employ multiple licensed architects. If you are hiring a firm rather than a solo practitioner, ask which licensed architect of record will be responsible for stamping the construction documents on your project. This person carries legal responsibility for the work and should not be someone you never meet.

Checking Disciplinary History

Beyond confirming an active license, check whether any complaints or disciplinary actions appear in the board's records. These are public. A single past complaint does not disqualify a candidate, but a pattern, or a complaint involving client funds, is a serious signal. Ask the architect directly if anything appears, and evaluate how they characterize it.

The right question to ask

“Which state licenses do you currently hold, and who is the architect of record on this project?” If the answer involves anyone other than the person you are meeting with, ask to meet that person before signing.

Reading a Portfolio Beyond Aesthetics

A portfolio is a curated marketing document. Its primary purpose is to show the firm's aesthetic range and creative capabilities. That is useful, but it tells you almost nothing about whether your project will be delivered on time, on budget, or without significant contractor disputes.

“The images you see on a firm's website represent the result. What you actually need to evaluate is the process that produced them.”

What a Portfolio Can Tell You

  • Whether the firm has completed projects similar in type, scale, and program to yours
  • Whether their design sensibility is consistent with what you are trying to achieve
  • How they handle materiality, detail, and site conditions across different projects
  • Whether their completed work photographs are of built projects or primarily renderings

What a Portfolio Cannot Tell You

  • Whether those projects were delivered within the original budget
  • Whether there were significant change orders or client disputes
  • Whether the lead designer you met is the person who executed the work shown
  • How the client actually experienced working with the firm

When reviewing a portfolio, ask the architect to walk you through two or three projects in detail—not a presentation slide, but a real conversation. Ask what the original budget was and what the final cost came in at. Ask whether there were scope changes initiated by the firm, and how those were documented.

Portfolio question to ask

“Of the projects shown in your portfolio, which ones were completed under budget, and which ran over? What happened?” A candid answer is a good sign. A defensive one is information too.

How to Check References Properly

Architects, like most service professionals, provide references who are likely to speak well of them. That does not make reference checks useless—it means you need to ask different questions than the ones the architect is expecting you to ask.

Request at least three references, and specifically ask for at least one from a project completed within the last two years and one where the project encountered a significant challenge—a budget overrun, a contractor problem, a permit delay. How a firm handles difficulty is more predictive than how it performs in ideal conditions.

Reference Questions That Reveal More Than the Usual Ones

Most clients are asked whether they would hire the architect again. These questions get at what that question misses.

Q “What was the original budget when you hired them, and what did the project actually cost to build?”
Architects rarely volunteer this information. The gap between early estimates and final construction cost is one of the most predictive data points available to you.
Q “When something went wrong—a contractor issue, a permit problem, an error in the drawings—how did the firm handle it?”
Every project encounters problems. The answer to this question tells you about accountability and communication under stress.
Q “Who from the firm did you actually deal with day-to-day? Was it the principal you hired, or someone else?”
In larger firms, the person who wins the project often hands it off to junior staff. This question surfaces that dynamic.
Q “Were there change orders you felt were avoidable? Were they clearly explained and documented in advance?”
Change orders are inevitable. Surprise change orders without prior communication are a process failure, not an inevitability.
Q “If you had to do it again, what would you do differently in how you worked with them?”
This framing invites honest reflection without asking the client to criticize directly. Listen for patterns.
One more step

Ask the reference whether you can see the project in person. Built projects reveal information that photographs do not: material quality, fit and finish, how the spaces actually feel. Most clients are willing to show a project they're proud of.

Project-Type and Scale Experience

Architecture is a broad discipline. A firm with a strong track record in single-family residential work is not necessarily equipped to manage a mixed-use development, and vice versa. The permit processes, contractor relationships, code requirements, and client dynamics differ significantly across project types.

What matters is not whether the firm has done exactly your project before, but whether they have demonstrated competency in the elements that make your project complex: its scale, occupancy type, site constraints, structural approach, or permitting jurisdiction.

The Project-Phase Competency Gap

Some firms are stronger in design phases—schematic design, design development—and weaker in technical execution phases: construction documents and construction administration. This gap is rarely mentioned in a pitch, but it is one of the most common sources of budget overruns and contractor disputes.

Request a sample construction document set from a completed project similar to yours. The quality, organization, and completeness of CD packages is one of the best predictors of how smoothly your project will be bid and built.

Scale and Budget Familiarity

An architect who has worked primarily on $400,000 residential renovations may not have the contractor relationships, consultant team, or process infrastructure for a $4 million new build. The reverse is also true: a large institutional firm may bring unnecessary overhead and overhead cost to a modest custom home.

Ask specifically: “What is the range of construction budgets for projects you have completed in the last three years?” A firm that regularly operates in your budget range is more likely to have calibrated expectations about what your money buys.

Evaluating Process Transparency

The single most common complaint about architects among clients who have been through difficult projects is not the design—it is the communication. Specifically: not understanding what they were agreeing to, not being told about cost implications before decisions were made, and not knowing where the project stood at any given time.

Process transparency is not a personality trait. It is a set of systems and habits that a firm either has or does not have. You can evaluate it before you sign a contract.

Questions That Reveal Process Quality

  • “How do you communicate project status to clients, and how often?” Look for a specific cadence, not a vague “we're very communicative.”
  • “When a design decision has cost implications, how do you communicate that to clients and when?” The answer should describe a proactive system, not a reactive one.
  • “Can you show me a sample of your meeting notes or construction administration reports?” Firms with strong process have these as standard documents. Firms without strong process often do not have them at all.
  • “How is the scope of your services defined in the contract, and what is not included?” An architect who can answer this clearly and in plain language is one who has thought carefully about scope.
The contract is part of process

Architects who use the AIA standard owner-architect agreement (or a clearly written custom contract) are signaling something about their professional seriousness. Architects who resist putting service scope in writing, or who use vague letter agreements, present a higher risk of scope disputes later.

Cost Estimating: Accountability vs. Avoidance

Early in a project, architects may not know what something will cost to build. That is understandable. What is not acceptable is consistently optimistic estimates that do not update as the design develops, or architects who decline to put any estimate on paper.

Ask: “Do you provide cost estimates at each design phase, and what happens when the contractor's bid comes in significantly higher than your estimate?” The willingness to answer this question directly tells you as much as the answer itself.

Who Will Actually Work on Your Project

In smaller firms, the principal you interview is likely the person who will do most of the work. In firms with five or more employees, that assumption no longer holds. Projects are often won by a principal with a strong portfolio and client presence, then handed to project architects and architectural staff who manage day-to-day execution.

This is not inherently problematic. Many firms are structured this way and deliver excellent results. What matters is whether you know what the structure is before you sign, and whether the people who will do the work have the experience relevant to your project.

Roles to Ask About

Principal / Partner
Signs the contract and stamps documents. Ask how frequently they will attend project meetings and review drawings at each phase.
Project Architect
Manages drawing production and contractor coordination. Often your primary day-to-day contact. Request a meeting before signing.
Architectural Staff
Produces drawings and models. Appropriate on many tasks, but ask who reviews their work and how errors in CDs are caught.
Consultant Network
Structural, MEP, civil, landscape. Ask who the consultants are and whether the architect has an established working relationship with them.

Ask: “Can I meet the project architect who will be assigned to my job before we sign a contract?” If the firm resists this, it is worth knowing why. If they agree, pay attention to how that person describes the firm's process from their own perspective.

Staff turnover is a hidden risk

Ask the firm about staff turnover. If the person assigned to your project leaves mid-construction—which happens—ask what the firm's handoff protocol is. How is project knowledge documented so that a new team member can pick it up without the client absorbing the cost of the transition?

Red Flags and Green Flags

After a series of interviews and reference checks, you will have a significant amount of qualitative information. These patterns help distinguish signals from noise.

“The evaluation conversation is itself a data point. An architect who is transparent, specific, and unhurried when answering hard questions before the contract is signed is more likely to behave that way during construction.”

Making the Decision

Architecture is a long engagement. A residential project can involve twelve to eighteen months of active collaboration before a single nail is driven. A commercial project longer. You are not just evaluating credentials—you are evaluating whether you can work with this person or firm through a sustained, high-stakes process that will test both parties.

Credentials, portfolio, and references answer the question of whether a firm is capable. Process transparency and staffing clarity answer the question of whether working with them is likely to be productive. Both matter. Neither alone is sufficient.

Verify First
Confirm active licensure in your project state. Check for disciplinary history. Identify the architect of record. Non-negotiable.
Dig Into Process
Portfolio shows capability. References, sample documents, and direct questions about past overruns reveal how that capability is actually deployed.
Know the Team
Meet the people who will work on your project, not just the person who pitched it. Understand consultant relationships and the handoff protocol if staff changes.
