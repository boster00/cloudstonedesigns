Before we begin

A first meeting is a conversation, not an audition.

Many people arrive at a first design consultation quietly worried: Am I too early in my planning? What if they ask about budget and I don’t know the answer? Do I need a mood board?

The short answer is no. A good first meeting is not a test of how prepared you are. It is the beginning of a listening process — one where the designer’s job is to understand you, not impress you with their portfolio.

That said, knowing what to expect makes the whole thing easier. So here is an honest, plain-language guide to what should happen in a first consultation and how we structure ours.

The best first meeting ends with you feeling clearer, not more overwhelmed. If you leave with more questions than you arrived with, something went wrong.

— A useful benchmark for any design conversation
What to expect

What a good first meeting covers

Every project is different, but a well-run first consultation follows a recognizable shape. These are the four things it should address — and one thing it should not.

Listening to your vision

A good designer asks open questions and spends most of the meeting listening. How do you use this space? How do you want it to feel? What’s not working right now? You do not need polished answers — halting, honest ones are more useful.

Asking about site, budget & timeline

Your designer will ask practical questions about your property, your approximate budget range, and when you need or want things completed. Ranges are fine. Honest uncertainty is better than invented precision.

Explaining their process

A confident design practice will clearly walk you through how they work: what the phases look like, how decisions get made, what happens with contractors, and what is expected of you at each step.

Discussing fit — not just fees

The conversation should touch on whether this is the right project for their practice and whether their approach matches what you actually need. Fit matters as much as talent.

Not a pitch

A first meeting should not be 45 minutes of portfolio slides, awards, or project lists. That is a sales presentation — and it puts the wrong person at the center of the room.

A note on preparation: You do not need a mood board, measurements, or a finalized brief. If you have photos or sketches you love, bring them. If you do not, that is perfectly fine — describing a feeling in words is just as useful to a skilled designer.
After the meeting

What you should walk away with

By the end of a first consultation, you should have enough to make a confident next decision — whether that’s moving forward, taking more time, or knowing this isn’t the right fit. Here is what that looks like in practice.

  • A clear sense of how this designer thinks and communicates — not just what they produce.
  • A realistic ballpark for what your project might cost, even if only a range.
  • An understanding of what the next formal step would be and roughly what it involves.
  • A felt sense — not just a logical one — of whether you felt heard in the conversation.
  • Answers to the practical questions you came in with, or a clear plan for when you will get them.

What to watch for

What a poor first meeting looks like

Not every first consultation is run well. Here are patterns that suggest a design practice may not be the right fit — or may not be operating in your best interest.

They do most of the talking

If the designer spends the meeting showcasing their work without asking about yours, that dynamic rarely improves once the project starts.

Budget questions are avoided

A designer who won’t discuss money in a first meeting is making later conversations harder, not easier. Transparency early is a sign of professionalism.

The process is vague or undefined

If you ask how they work and get a general answer like “we just make it happen,” that is not modesty — it is an absence of a system you can rely on.

You feel pressured to decide quickly

Good designers do not manufacture urgency. If you leave feeling rushed or that the opportunity will disappear, pay attention to that feeling.

Your questions are deflected

Every reasonable question you ask in a first meeting should get a direct answer, or an honest “I’ll find out and send it to you.” Deflection is a pattern, not a one-off.

No written follow-up

Memory fades and details blur. If a designer doesn’t send a brief written summary after a first meeting, consider how documentation will be handled throughout a full project.

If any of these sound familiar from a past experience, you are not alone — and you are not wrong to have noticed. The standard can be higher. A first consultation should leave you feeling informed and respected, not sold to.
How we work

How Cloudstone Designs structures its initial project conversation

Our first meeting is a 60-minute conversation, not a presentation. Here is the shape of it — so there are no surprises when you walk in.

1

Minutes 0 – 5

We start with context, not credentials.

We briefly explain who we are and how we work — not to impress, but so you understand the frame. Then we hand the conversation over to you.

2

Minutes 5 – 25

We listen to your project and your life.

We ask about the space, how you use it, what frustrates you about it now, and what you imagine for it. We ask about your household, your timeline, and the intangibles — how a space should feel, not just what it should look like.

3

Minutes 25 – 40

We talk practically: budget, timeline, and scope.

We ask about your approximate investment range — not to hold you to it, but to make sure we are thinking about your project realistically from the start. We discuss your timeline, any fixed constraints, and the likely scope of work.

4

Minutes 40 – 55

We walk you through what working with us looks like.

We explain our phases, how decisions get made, who you’ll be in contact with, and what is expected of you at each stage. We answer your questions directly.

5

Minutes 55 – 60

We close with next steps, not a close.

We tell you what happens next if you want to move forward, and we mean it when we say there is no pressure. You should have time to think. We follow up in writing the same day.

What we do not do

We do not bring a contract to a first meeting. We do not ask you to decide on the spot. We do not charge for the initial conversation. If we are not the right fit for your project, we will say so honestly — and point you somewhere useful if we can.
