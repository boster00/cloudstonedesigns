01 The big picture

The Cost Iceberg: What the Quote Doesn't Include

When most clients receive a contractor's bid, they treat it as the budget. It isn't. A construction quote covers the cost of building the thing. It rarely covers the cost of getting permission to build it, coordinating the consultants who designed it, furnishing it, or managing the process.

The visible tip of the iceberg — the GC's contract sum — is typically 60–75% of total project cost on a well-managed build. The remaining 25–40% lives below the waterline, spread across categories that are entirely predictable once you know where to look.

02 Soft costs

Consultant Fees Nobody Budgets For

Design is a team sport. Your architect of record is typically the conductor, but the orchestra is made up of engineers and specialists — each of whom bills separately. These fees are almost never included in a GC's construction bid. They accrue before ground breaks and continue through construction.

Consultant fee ranges as a percentage of construction cost
Consultant Typical Range Notes
Structural Engineer 1–3% of construction cost Higher on complex systems, hillside, or seismic-sensitive sites
MEP Engineer Often omitted 2–5% of construction cost Mechanical, electrical, plumbing. Often skipped on residential; always needed on commercial
Civil Engineer $4,000–$25,000+ Required for grading, drainage, new site work, or anything touching the street
Landscape Architect 8–15% of landscape budget Separate from general contractor; often added late and over budget
Geotechnical / Soils Frequently missed $3,000–$12,000 Required by most jurisdictions for new foundations; non-negotiable
Acoustical / AV / Technology $5,000–$40,000+ Depends on scope; often value-engineered out and later regretted

What to ask: When reviewing any design contract, ask explicitly which consultants are included in the fee and which are "additional services" or "owner-direct." A well-structured proposal will name them all. One that doesn't is leaving the cost for you to discover.

03 Government fees

Permits, Plan Check, and Government Fees

Permit fees are public record, but almost no one looks them up before budgeting. The range is enormous — from a few hundred dollars for a simple interior alteration to six figures for a large new commercial building. The cost is set by the jurisdiction, based on project valuation, occupancy type, and construction type.

What surprises most clients isn't the permit fee itself. It's the plan check fee, the multiple round-trip resubmittal cycle, the school fees, the fire department clearance, and the utility company connection fees that arrive as separate invoices, months apart.

  • Building permit fee (typically 0.5–2% of project valuation)
  • Plan check fee (often 65–80% of the permit fee, paid upfront)
  • Expedite fee if fast-tracking is required
  • Fire and life safety plan check (separate department, separate fee)
  • School impact fees (California: $5–$9 per square foot of new area)
  • Utility connection and upgrade fees (gas, electric, water, sewer)
  • State-mandated energy and green building compliance review
  • Health department fee for any food-service or medical occupancy

Diagram Placeholder

Permit timeline: Submittal → Plan Check (4–12 weeks) → Corrections → Resubmittal → Approval → Permit Issuance → Inspections during construction.

Rule of thumb: budget 1.5–3% of total construction cost for all government fees on a mid-size project. In California, add school fees separately. In jurisdictions with slow plan check turnarounds, also budget holding costs if your project is time-sensitive.

04 Third-party costs

Testing, Inspections, and Third-Party Oversight

The building department's inspector visits a project a handful of times. But for projects with concrete, steel, high-strength masonry, or special inspections required by the structural engineer, you'll need a third-party Special Inspection program — a separate firm hired and paid by the owner, present during critical work phases.

Similarly, commissioning agents verify that mechanical and electrical systems perform as designed. Lenders and some municipalities require them. Either way, they catch expensive problems before walls are closed.

  • Soils compaction testing during grading and foundation work
  • Concrete testing: cylinder breaks, slump, air content per batch
  • Structural steel inspection and bolt torque verification
  • High-strength masonry and epoxy anchor inspection
  • Special inspection program fees: $8,000–$50,000+ on commercial projects
  • Mechanical and plumbing systems commissioning
  • Building envelope / waterproofing testing (especially below grade)
  • Air barrier and energy code compliance testing
  • Hazardous materials (asbestos, lead) survey and clearance on existing buildings

These costs are paid by the owner, not the contractor. A GC who tells you "inspections are included" either means their own quality-control visits (which have no regulatory standing) or is omitting the third-party program entirely. Ask which type they mean.

05 Furnishings & equipment

FF&E: The Furnishings Budget That Gets Forgotten

FF&E stands for Furniture, Fixtures, and Equipment. It covers everything that isn't permanently attached to the building — and on a typical project, it can represent 15–25% of total project cost, often more.

The disconnect happens because construction bids address the shell and core: walls, floors, ceilings, built-ins, and mechanical systems. The moment a client walks in expecting to occupy the space, they encounter a list of purchases nobody priced: seating, case goods, light fixtures, appliances, technology, art, window treatments, and signage.

"We've seen projects come in $300,000 under their construction budget and $400,000 over their total budget because nobody priced the furniture until move-in month."

What belongs in an FF&E budget

  • Freestanding furniture (seating, tables, storage, beds)
  • Decorative light fixtures (pendants, sconces, floor lamps) — typically excluded from MEP
  • Appliances: residential and commercial kitchen equipment
  • Window treatments: shades, curtains, blinds
  • Audio-visual and technology (TVs, displays, speakers, control systems)
  • Art and accessories
  • Signage and wayfinding (commercial and hospitality)
  • Small equipment: commercial kitchen smallwares, fitness equipment, etc.
  • Delivery, installation, and freight

When to budget it: At schematic design, alongside construction cost. Not after construction bids come in. Waiting creates false confidence and forces compromises at the worst possible time.

06 Project overhead

Contingency, Owner's Rep, and Project Management

Contingency

Contingency is not a sign of bad planning. It is the product of honest planning. Every construction project encounters unknowns: existing conditions that differ from drawings, owner-initiated scope changes, supply chain delays, unforeseen subsurface conditions. The question isn't whether contingency will be needed. It's whether you've budgeted for it.

Conceptual / Early Design 15–20%
Design Development 10–15%
Construction Documents Complete 7–10%
During Construction 5%

Owner's Representative

An owner's rep is a construction-experienced advocate working exclusively for the client. They review applications for payment, track schedule, flag quality issues, and manage the relationship between the owner, designer, and contractor. On projects above $1M, an owner's rep typically saves far more than their fee. Expect 2–5% of construction cost.

Project Management Overhead

Even if you don't hire a formal owner's rep, your own time managing vendors, making decisions, attending site visits, and reviewing submittals has a real cost. For owner-operators building out commercial space, factor in staff time, temporary relocation, business interruption, and moving costs — none of which appear in any contractor's bid.

07 Due diligence

How to Read a Fee Proposal for Hidden Exclusions

Most fee proposals are written to win, not to inform. Exclusions are buried in small print. Scope limitations are phrased as inclusions. The following are the most common places where cost exposure hides in plain sight.

Diagram Placeholder

Annotated proposal visual: highlight clauses around "exclusions," "additional services," "owner-direct consultants," and "allowances."

  1. 1

    "Allowances" without a clear dollar amount

    An allowance is a placeholder. If a proposal says "tile allowance included" without specifying the dollar value, you have no idea what's actually covered. Ask for the number.

  2. 2

    "Owner-direct" consultant language

    Phrases like "owner to procure directly" or "civil/MEP by others" mean that consultant's fee is entirely outside this contract. It is not a small cost — budget for it immediately.

  3. 3

    Permit fees "estimated" or "not included"

    Permit fees are public record. Any firm with local experience can produce a reasonable estimate. "Not included" or a vague lump sum is a flag worth investigating.

  4. 4

    "Reimbursable expenses" without a cap

    Printing, mileage, travel, model-making, and consultant sub-fees marked as reimbursable with no cap can add thousands to a fixed-fee contract over a long project.

  5. 5

    No mention of resubmittal rounds for permitting

    Most jurisdictions require at least one correction cycle. If the proposal covers one submittal only, you may be paying hourly for every round after the first. Ask how many rounds are included.

  6. 6

    Construction administration described as "as needed"

    Construction administration is when cost overruns and quality problems are caught and corrected. "As needed" shifts the burden of determining whether CA is needed to the contractor — not the firm working for you.

08 Cost of changes

What Changes Cost During Construction

The single most controllable driver of budget overruns is the owner-initiated change order. The timing of a decision matters as much as the scope of the change. The same decision made at three different phases can cost very different amounts.

  • Design Changes cost time, not money. Revising a drawing is cheap. Moving a wall on paper takes hours. This is the moment to make decisions.
  • Permitted CDs Changes require a revision to permitted drawings, possible resubmittal to the building department, and updates across multiple consultants. Plan for $1,500–$10,000 per significant revision, plus a schedule delay.
  • After GMP / Bid The contractor has priced the work as drawn. Anything outside that scope is a change order, typically at a 15–20% markup over direct cost. No competitive pressure means no market check on pricing.
  • Work in Place If the work is already built, change it means demolition, disposal, rework, and re-inspection. A $3,000 framing decision made during design can cost $18,000 as a change order to existing work.
  • Owner-Supplied Items If the owner supplies materials directly (tile, fixtures, appliances), the contractor will charge for coordination, delay caused by late delivery, and any resulting change to the schedule. Coordinate delivery dates to the day.

The cheapest change order is the one you made six months earlier, on a drawing, during design development.

Transparency with your design team about scope, selection timelines, and budget limits during design is the most effective cost-control tool available. It's not glamorous. It works.
