01 — What the Experience Did to You

Being Burned Sharpens Some Instinctsand Distorts Others

A bad engagement with a creative firm leaves marks that do not fade quickly. You learned things — real things — about how these relationships can go wrong. That knowledge is legitimate and worth keeping. But the experience also tends to produce a set of defensive instincts that, however understandable, can make the next engagement harder to get right.

The challenge is knowing which responses to trust and which ones to examine.

Keep these instincts

The ones the experience sharpened rightly

  • Scrutinizing process documentation before signing
  • Asking for references from clients with projects similar to yours
  • Noticing when answers to direct questions stay vague
  • Wanting clear ownership of deliverables spelled out in writing
  • Trusting a quiet sense that chemistry is off, even if you cannot name why
Examine these carefully

The ones the experience created that can work against you

  • Assuming a larger firm is automatically safer than a smaller one
  • Letting the cheapest proposal feel the least risky because you lose less
  • Over-indexing on portfolio aesthetics at the expense of process
  • Wanting such tight control that collaboration becomes impossible
  • Writing off any firm that resembles the previous one superficially
A note on hypervigilance

There is a version of due diligence that is genuinely protective, and there is a version that is so thorough that no real firm can pass it. The goal is not to eliminate all risk. The goal is to reduce the specific risks that burned you before, while leaving room for a good partnership to form.

02 — Reading the Room Accurately

What a Genuinely Trustworthy Firm Looks Likevs. One That Just Markets Trust Well

The firms that burned the most clients are often not the ones with the worst portfolios — they are the ones with the best sales process. A polished deck, smooth discovery calls, and confident language about process can all be produced without the underlying capability or care to back them up. Here is how to tell the difference.

03 — Due Diligence That Actually Works

The Questions That Expose Whether a FirmHas Learned From Past Client Problems

The purpose of these questions is not to catch anyone out. It is to make space for an honest conversation. A firm that takes them seriously is one worth continuing to talk to. A firm that treats them as an obstacle to close the deal is giving you important information quickly and cheaply.

  1. "Can you tell me about a project that went significantly off-track and how you handled it?"

    Every firm of any age has one. If they cannot or will not describe a real example, that absence tells you something. If they describe one honestly — including their own contribution to the problem — that tells you something better.

  2. "Who specifically will be working on my project day to day, and how much of their time?"

    Bait-and-switch staffing — where the senior person sells the work and juniors deliver it — is a common source of client disappointment. Ask for names, not titles.

  3. "What is the most common reason your projects go over budget or timeline, and what have you changed as a result?"

    This forces a structural answer, not a one-off story. A firm that has thought seriously about this will have a real pattern to describe and a genuine response to it.

  4. "Can I speak with a client whose project had complications, not just your best-case references?"

    This request is unusual enough that most firms are not prepared for it. A confident firm with integrity will accommodate it. The conversation with that reference will be more useful than six glowing testimonials.

  5. "What do you do when a client is unhappy with a direction, even after several revision rounds?"

    The answer reveals how the firm handles conflict, whether they see disagreement as a problem to manage or information to use, and what the off-ramp looks like if things do not work out.

  6. "If we are three months in and both parties feel the fit is not right, how does that conversation happen and what does exit look like?"

    A firm that has a clear, fair answer to this question has thought about client relationships with honesty. Resistance to the question is a warning sign worth noting.

04 — When to Walk Away Early

Red Flags in the First MeetingThat Should Make You Pause

The first meeting is not just a chance for the firm to present themselves. It is the closest thing you will get to seeing how they operate when the stakes are still low. Pay attention to what you observe in the room, not just what they say.

  • They spend more time talking than asking

    A firm that has done this long enough knows that discovery takes longer than presenting. If the first meeting is mostly their slide deck and very few questions about your situation, that ratio tends to persist into the project.

  • Every competitor they mention is subtly incompetent

    Firms that build their positioning entirely around what others do wrong tend to reflect poorly on the industry as a way to avoid scrutiny of their own work. Genuine confidence sounds different from constant contrast.

  • They respond to your concerns by minimizing them

    If you share what went wrong last time and the response is some version of "that would not happen with us," without any curiosity about the specifics, take note. Firms that learn from clients start by actually listening to them.

  • Urgency appears in the pricing conversation

    "This rate is only available until Friday" or "we have one team opening left" are sales mechanisms, not facts about a firm's capacity. Trustworthy firms do not manufacture pressure. They let the work speak.

  • The proposal arrives before you have finished talking

    A proposal that lands the same afternoon as the first call was written largely before that call. You are being fit into a template, not genuinely scoped. That efficiency has a cost that usually shows up in misaligned expectations.

  • They cannot explain their own contract simply

    If you ask what happens to your files if you end the relationship early and the answer is complicated or uncertain, that clause will matter at a moment when you have far less leverage than you have right now.

05 — A Safer Way to Start

How to Structure a Limited-Scope Engagementto Test Fit Before Full Commitment

You do not have to choose between not hiring anyone and hiring someone for a twelve-month retainer. There is a middle path: a bounded, scoped pilot that gives both parties useful information without the full exposure of a major contract. Good firms will welcome this. Firms that resist it are telling you something.

1

Choose a contained, real-stakes deliverable

Select something small enough to complete in four to six weeks but meaningful enough that you can evaluate the output honestly. A brand standards document, a single campaign creative direction, or a UX audit are good candidates. Artificial exercises tell you less than real work.

2

Define success criteria before you start

Write down what a successful outcome looks like — specifically, not vaguely — before the engagement begins. Share this document with the firm and ask them to respond to it. How they engage with that document is itself a signal about how they will engage with the project.

3

Treat every interaction as data

Are they on time? Do they communicate proactively when something changes, or wait for you to ask? Do they present their thinking or just their output? The behaviours that emerge in a small engagement under low pressure are the same ones you will see scaled up in a large one under real pressure.

4

Schedule a mid-point check-in with the principal

At the halfway point, have a direct conversation with whoever leads the firm or the account. Not a status call — a candid conversation about how you both feel about how it is going. This gives you a chance to raise concerns while the relationship is still forming and there is room to course-correct.

5

Make the decision to continue a deliberate one

When the pilot ends, do not let momentum carry you into the next phase automatically. Pause. Review what you observed against what you hoped to see. If something felt off, name it before the next contract is signed, not after.

On what this costs you

A pilot engagement will cost more per deliverable than the same work inside a longer contract. That premium is the cost of information. You are paying to learn something about the firm before committing to a larger relationship. That is not inefficient. That is the right use of a limited budget given your situation.
