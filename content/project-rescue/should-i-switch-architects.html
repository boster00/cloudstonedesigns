01 — Diagnosis

Before You Decide Anything: Is This Problem Fixable?

Most clients who are thinking about switching architects have been thinking about it for a while. They’ve raised concerns, seen temporary improvement, and then watched the situation drift back. By the time the question becomes urgent, the emotional cost of staying and the practical cost of leaving feel roughly equivalent — and neither path feels good.

That’s exactly where clear thinking matters most, and where it’s hardest to find. This guide won’t tell you to switch. It will help you think through whether you should.

“The question isn’t whether your current situation is frustrating. It’s whether the source of that frustration is something the relationship can actually fix.”

Start by separating the problem from the person. Some of the most common complaints — slow responses, design revisions that miss the mark, contractor confusion — can stem from either a fixable process breakdown or an embedded incompatibility in how you and your architect work. The diagnosis determines everything.

Run this diagnostic first

For each statement, be honest about whether it describes your situation:

  • You have had a direct, explicit conversation about the problem — not just hints or email threads — and the architect acknowledged it.
  • There was a period — at project start or at another point — when the relationship worked and you felt confident in the process.
  • The problems are specific and describable (missed deadlines, scope not reflected in drawings) rather than a general feeling of unease.
  • The problems have persisted through multiple billing cycles and multiple promises to improve, without meaningful change.
  • You find yourself preparing for conversations with your architect the way you might prepare for a difficult negotiation.
  • The contractor or other consultants have started coming to you directly because they can’t get reliable answers from the design team.

Green items are signals that something fixable may have broken down. Red items are signals that the problem may be structural. If your list is mostly red, keep reading.

02 — The Real Question

Situational Breakdown vs. Structural Mismatch

Not all bad project experiences have the same cause, and the cause determines whether staying — and resetting — is realistic.

Situational breakdowns often have identifiable causes: the project moved into a more complex phase, a key team member left the firm, or scope crept in ways that weren’t managed well. These can sometimes be corrected through a direct conversation, a formal reset meeting, or a renegotiated scope of services. The underlying working relationship is intact.

Structural mismatches are different. They usually involve a fundamental disconnect in values, working style, or what each party expects from the other. Signs include: your architect consistently defends decisions rather than listening, the design keeps drifting away from what you actually asked for, or there is a persistent gap between what is promised and what is delivered — regardless of who on their team you deal with.

Worth noting

A structural mismatch rarely improves after one reset conversation. If you have already had that conversation and are reading this guide, you are likely facing a structural problem — not a situational one.

One honest way to test this: ask yourself whether you trust your architect’s judgment on things you can’t verify yourself — structural decisions, code compliance, contractor coordination. If the answer is no, that is a more serious problem than any individual complaint.

03 — Full Cost Accounting

The Real Costs of Switching. Don’t Minimize Them.

We want to be direct: switching architects mid-project is genuinely disruptive. Any firm that tells you otherwise is overselling the transition. These are the costs you should plan for before you decide anything.

Cost Category
What It Means in Practice

Documentation Handoff

Your incoming architect will need to review everything produced so far: drawings, specifications, permit applications, consultant coordination, emails, meeting notes. Gaps in documentation are common and require investigative time to resolve.

Fee Duplication

Expect to pay for work twice in some areas. A new architect cannot simply pick up where another left off without re-examining assumptions. Schematic-level work may need to be redone even if the drawings look complete.

Schedule Impact

A transition typically adds 6–14 weeks to a project, depending on phase and complexity. If you are pre-permit, the impact is usually more manageable. Post-permit transitions are significantly more complicated.

Contractor Confusion

Contractors working from existing drawings will have questions about intent. A new architect inheriting drawings they didn’t produce will need time to understand the design logic before they can answer those questions reliably.

Consultant Relationships

Structural, mechanical, and civil engineers often have working relationships with the original architect. Re-coordinating these relationships, or bringing in new consultants, adds overhead and potential coordination gaps.

Psychological Cost

Starting over with a new relationship after a difficult one takes energy. You will need to rebuild trust, re-explain your priorities, and re-establish communication norms. This is real work, even when it goes well.

“The disruption of switching is real. The question is whether it is greater than the cost of staying in a relationship that is not working.”

Weigh these costs against what continuing the current relationship is costing you: in delays already incurred, in rework already paid for, and in the compounding risk of a project that is not being properly managed. Sometimes the math favors switching. But do the math honestly.

04 — Before You Move

How to Protect Yourself Legally Before You Leave

If you have decided to make a change, the steps you take before giving notice matter significantly. Acting carelessly in this phase can limit your rights, complicate the transition, and create disputes that follow the project for years.

Read your contract before you do anything else

Most architectural agreements — especially AIA contracts — contain specific provisions about termination for convenience, required notice periods (typically 7–14 days written notice), and what happens to documents, fees, and intellectual property upon termination. Know what you agreed to before you act on it.

Understand document ownership and licensing

In most standard contracts, the architect retains ownership of the drawings as instruments of professional service. You may have a limited license to use them to complete the project — but only if you are not in default on fees. Unpaid invoices can complicate your access to your own project documents. Settle outstanding fees, or negotiate their settlement, before terminating.

Gather and secure your records independently

Before giving any notice, gather your own copies of all project correspondence, meeting minutes, permit applications, drawings you have received, and any approvals. Once a relationship turns adversarial, access to records can become contested. Do not rely on the architect’s file as your primary record.

Consider a brief legal consultation

If the project involves significant fees, disputes about work quality, or permits that are already issued, a single hour with a construction attorney familiar with architectural contracts can clarify your position and prevent costly mistakes. This is not about litigation — it is about knowing your rights before you negotiate.

Give formal written notice

Termination should be in writing, should reference the relevant contract clause, and should state the effective date clearly. Do not terminate verbally or by implication. A clean, professional notice protects you legally and sets the right tone for whatever negotiations follow.

Important

We are an architecture firm, not a legal practice. The above is practical guidance, not legal advice. If your project involves disputes over fees, quality of work, or permit complications, consult a licensed attorney before terminating your agreement.

05 — The Right Way Forward

What a Good Transition Actually Looks Like

Most transition problems are caused not by the complexity of the changeover itself, but by doing it reactively — without planning, without proper documentation, and without a clear brief for the incoming architect. A well-managed transition looks different.

“A good transition is deliberate, documented, and honest about what the incoming architect is inheriting.”

The incoming architect’s first task should be a thorough review of existing documents — not to validate them, but to understand them. Any competent architect taking over a project will want to assess: what decisions have been locked in by permits or contractor agreements, what is still open, where the documentation is solid, and where there are gaps or ambiguities.

A formal project handoff meeting between the departing and incoming architect is ideal, though not always possible if the relationship has become adversarial. If it is not possible, a written project status summary from the outgoing architect (which you can request as part of termination) is a reasonable substitute.

Contractor communication needs to be proactive. Your contractor should be informed of the transition in writing before it happens, not after. They should know who their new point of contact is, that existing drawings remain valid unless revised, and how RFIs and submittals will be handled during the transition window.

06 — Clear Cases

When Staying Is Clearly the Wrong Choice

Most situations exist in a gray zone where a thoughtful cost-benefit analysis applies. But some situations are not gray. The following are circumstances where the calculus is clearer — where staying is likely to compound the problem rather than resolve it.

Signals that staying may be possible

  • The problem is isolated to a specific phase or team member, not the firm as a whole
  • Your architect has taken concrete, verifiable steps to address the issue
  • You are post-permit and the transition cost is genuinely prohibitive
  • The design work is strong even if communication has been poor
  • A structured reset meeting has not yet been attempted

Signals that leaving is likely necessary

  • There are errors in permitted documents that your architect will not acknowledge or correct
  • The architect has stopped responding to calls, emails, or contractor RFIs without explanation
  • You have discovered that work billed has not been completed as described
  • The relationship has become openly adversarial and productive conversation is no longer possible
  • The contractor has lost confidence in the design documentation and is proceeding on assumptions
  • You genuinely do not trust the architect’s professional judgment

The last item on the right column is the most important one. Architecture involves a continuous stream of decisions that you cannot fully evaluate yourself. If you no longer trust the person making them, the cost of staying is not just relational — it is structural, financial, and potentially legal. Trust, once genuinely lost in a professional relationship, rarely returns.

“If you no longer trust your architect’s judgment on things you cannot verify yourself, that is not a communication problem. It is a fundamental problem with the relationship.”
