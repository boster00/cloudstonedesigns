Context

Most communication failures are structural, not personal

When a project goes sideways due to miscommunication, the instinct is to blame personalities. But in most cases the problem is the absence of a formal communication system. Good firms build one in from the start. Here is what that system is supposed to include — and what you should be able to expect from any qualified firm you hire.

Meeting cadence

A defined schedule of owner-architect meetings tied to project milestones — not ad hoc calls when something goes wrong.

Written summaries

Every meeting should produce a written follow-up that documents what was discussed, what was decided, and who owns each action item.

Decision log

A running record of every material, specification, and scope decision made — with dates and signatures. This is your protection and theirs.

RFI process

Requests for Information from the contractor must follow a defined workflow with documented responses — not a text thread with the project manager.

Change order process

Every scope or cost change must be documented, priced, and approved in writing before work begins. Verbal approvals are not change orders.

Phase sign-off

At the completion of each design phase, you should formally review and approve the work before the team advances. No silent progressions.

Standard 01 — Meeting Rhythm

The communication calendar a project should have

A well-run architecture engagement has a predictable meeting structure. The following is a baseline — your project may warrant more frequent touchpoints during complex phases.

Meeting type Frequency Who attends What it produces Phase
Design progress review Bi-weekly Owner, principal architect, project manager Written meeting minutes, updated decision log Design
Phase completion review Once per phase Owner, full design team Signed phase approval, written scope summary Design
Pre-construction meeting Once Owner, architect, general contractor, key subs Documented meeting minutes, contact directory, schedule baseline Construction
Site observation visit Weekly (minimum) Architect or project architect Written field report with photos, action items, RFI status Construction
OAC meeting Bi-weekly Owner, architect, general contractor Meeting minutes, schedule update, open RFI log, pending change orders Construction
Substantial completion walkthrough Once Owner, architect, GC Punch list, certificate of substantial completion Closeout

Note: An OAC (Owner-Architect-Contractor) meeting is a standard tri-party coordination call. If your firm does not hold them, ask why.

Standard 02 — Phase Documentation

What a good architect produces at each project phase

The AIA defines five standard phases of architectural services. Each phase should generate a specific set of documents. If your project concluded a phase without these, that is a gap — not a stylistic choice.

Standard 03 — Below-Standard Signals

How to tell whether your firm's communication is below standard

These are not edge cases. They are the most common patterns we see when clients come to us after a difficult experience with another firm.

  • 1

    No written follow-up after meetings

    If meetings produce decisions but no written record, those decisions will later be disputed. "We discussed it in the meeting" is not a substitute for documented minutes. A standard firm sends a summary within 48 hours of every meeting.

  • 2

    Change orders presented for signature after work is done

    A change order is an agreement to perform additional or modified work at a specified price. If you are being asked to sign one after the work is already in the wall, the process broke down. Approval should precede execution.

  • 3

    You cannot get a straight answer on where the budget stands

    At any point during design or construction, you should be able to ask your architect for the current opinion of probable cost and get a written response within a week. Vague answers about "waiting for contractor feedback" are a deflection, not an answer.

  • 4

    RFIs are handled by phone or text instead of a written log

    When a contractor calls with a field question and gets a verbal answer, and no one writes it down, you now have an undocumented interpretation of the construction documents. If it turns out to be wrong, there is no record of who said what.

  • 5

    You advanced from one phase to the next without a formal review

    Phase boundaries exist for a reason: they are the last low-cost opportunity to catch problems before they are locked in. If your project went from Schematic Design to Design Development to CDs without a formal review and sign-off at each stage, decisions were made that should have been yours to make.

  • 6

    You do not know who is actually working on your project

    A principal-in-charge who attends the initial meeting but then delegates entirely to junior staff without telling you is a common source of unmet expectations. You should know your project team by name, role, and seniority from day one.

  • 7

    Site visits are infrequent or not documented

    During construction, architecture is primarily about observing, interpreting, and responding. Weekly visits are standard on active construction. If your architect visits monthly and produces no written report, they are not performing construction administration — they are making an appearance.

What good practice looks like

  • Meeting minutes sent within 48 hours
  • Decision log updated after every design session
  • Change orders written, priced, and signed before work starts
  • Weekly field reports with photos
  • RFI log shared with owner on request
  • Phase sign-off documents in client file

What poor practice looks like

  • Verbal summaries at the next meeting — if at all
  • Decisions reconstructed from memory or email threads
  • Change orders signed under time pressure after work is done
  • Site visits undocumented or absent
  • RFIs handled by phone, no record kept
  • Project advances without formal approval
Standard 04 — Formal Processes

How the RFI and change order processes are supposed to work

These two processes are the most commonly mismanaged on construction projects. Understanding how they should work gives you leverage when they do not.

Request for Information (RFI)

Field process

An RFI is a formal written question from the contractor to the architect, seeking clarification about the construction documents. It is a normal part of every project — not a sign that something went wrong.

  1. 1 Contractor submits RFI in writing, referencing the drawing or spec section in question, with a response-requested date.
  2. 2 Architect logs and tracks the RFI with a unique number, issue date, and deadline.
  3. 3 Architect responds in writing — typically within 7 working days — with a clear, documented answer.
  4. 4 Response is archived in the project file and shared with all relevant parties, including the owner if the response involves cost or scope.
Watch for: Architects who respond by phone or on-site conversation without logging the response. An undocumented RFI response is legally equivalent to no answer at all.

Change Order (CO)

Cost & scope process

A change order is a written amendment to the construction contract that modifies scope, cost, or schedule. It requires agreement from the owner, architect, and contractor before it is binding.

  1. 1 Change is identified — by owner request, unforeseen condition, or coordination issue. A Construction Change Directive (CCD) may be issued to authorize urgent work pending pricing.
  2. 2 Contractor submits pricing in writing with labor, materials, and schedule impact itemized.
  3. 3 Architect reviews the pricing for reasonableness and issues a written recommendation to the owner.
  4. 4 Owner approves or negotiates the change order in writing before work proceeds.
  5. 5 Signed change order is filed and the contract sum and schedule are officially updated.
Watch for: Change orders presented in bulk at the end of a phase for signature. If you are signing multiple undated change orders at once, the process was not followed as work progressed.
Standard 05 — Evaluation Questions

What to ask a firm you are considering hiring

These questions are designed to surface communication discipline, not personality. A firm with good process will answer them without hesitation. Vague answers are themselves data.

01 "Can you walk me through exactly what you send me after every meeting?"

This tests whether meeting follow-up is a defined habit or an improvised courtesy. You want to hear a specific format: a written summary with date, attendees, decisions made, open items, and owner-required actions.

What a good answer sounds like"We send a written meeting summary within 48 hours. It documents the decisions made, who is responsible for each action item, and any items requiring your approval. We keep a running decision log throughout the project."
02 "How do you handle RFIs during construction, and will I have access to the RFI log?"

A firm without a defined RFI process will answer this question slowly. You want to understand the submission format, target response time, and whether RFIs that affect scope or cost are flagged to you before a response is issued.

What a good answer sounds like"RFIs are submitted in writing through our project management platform. We log every RFI with a number and target response date. Our standard response time is 7 working days. Any RFI that could affect your cost or scope is flagged to you before we respond."
03 "What does a change order look like in your process — and at what point do I sign one?"

The answer you do not want: "We collect them and send them to you periodically." Change orders should be presented as they occur, with itemized pricing and a recommendation, before work is performed.

What a good answer sounds like"When a change is identified, we request itemized pricing from the contractor and review it for reasonableness before presenting it to you with a written recommendation. You sign before the work proceeds. We never present retroactive change orders."
04 "How many site visits per month are included in your CA scope, and what is in your field report?"

Construction administration is routinely underscoped and underfunded. Ask for a number. Ask to see a sample field report. A field report without photos and open-item tracking is a social visit, not professional oversight.

What a good answer sounds like"We include weekly site visits during active construction. Each visit produces a written field report with construction status, photos, any work that does not conform to the documents, and the current status of open RFIs and submittals. You receive a copy."
05 "How do you conduct the phase review at the end of Schematic Design and Design Development?"

This question checks whether phase gates are real events or calendar fiction. A well-run firm has a defined review meeting, a checklist of deliverables, and a written approval before the team advances.

What a good answer sounds like"At the end of each phase we schedule a formal review meeting where we present the complete deliverable package. We give you a written summary of what the phase produced and what decisions were made. We do not advance to the next phase until you have signed the phase completion document."
06 "Who will actually be working on my project, and what is their role?"

This surfaces the bait-and-switch pattern common in larger firms. You want names, roles, and seniority. Ask specifically how much time the principal will personally spend on your project versus delegating to project staff.

What a good answer sounds like"Here is your project team: the principal-in-charge, a licensed project architect, and a project coordinator. I will personally review the design at each phase gate and attend every OAC meeting. Day-to-day coordination is handled by our project architect, whom you will speak with directly."
07 "Can I see a sample of your OAC meeting minutes from a current project?"

Asking to see an actual document is the fastest way to evaluate documentation quality. Hesitation to share a redacted sample is itself informative. A firm proud of its process will produce one immediately.

What a good answer sounds like"Absolutely — let me pull a redacted copy. Our OAC minutes include the date, attendees, the current schedule update, the status of open RFIs and submittals, pending and approved change orders, and action items with owners and due dates."
