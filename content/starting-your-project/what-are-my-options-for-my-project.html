Why This Decision Matters

You Are About to Lock In More Than a Timeline

The delivery method you select determines who controls design, who holds the contracts, who carries the risk, and how much you pay when something changes. Most owners decide this too late.

01

Budget Exposure

Some methods fix the cost early. Others pass risk back to you. The difference can be hundreds of thousands of dollars on a mid-size project.

02

Design Control

In some structures the architect answers to you. In others, they answer to the contractor. That changes what gets optimized: your priorities or the builder's margin.

03

Speed vs. Quality

Compressing design time can reduce fees. It also increases the chance of costly field changes, permit delays, and disputes. There is always a trade-off.

The Three Main Methods

Project Delivery Methods, Explained

These are the structures used on the vast majority of commercial, institutional, and larger residential projects. Each has a distinct logic about who does what and when.

Design-Bid-Build
Traditional Sequential Delivery
Also called: Stipulated Sum, Hard Bid

The architect designs the project completely before any contractor is involved. Once construction documents are finished, the owner solicits competitive bids. The lowest qualified bid wins, and a separate construction contract is signed.

This method gives you maximum design control and competitive pricing. The architect acts entirely as your agent throughout both design and construction, reviewing shop drawings, certifying payments, and tracking compliance against the documents they produced.

Best for: projects where quality and scope definition matter most, publicly funded work requiring transparent bidding, and owners who want an independent advocate during construction.

Design-Build
Single-Entity Delivery
Also called: Turnkey, EPC (Engineering, Procurement, Construction)

A single entity — either a contractor-led team or a design-build firm — holds both the design and construction contract. The architect is a subcontractor or in-house employee of the design-builder, not your agent.

You get a single point of accountability and faster delivery because design and construction overlap. The trade-off: the architect's primary obligation is to the design-builder's cost and schedule, not to your design vision. Value-engineering decisions may not favor quality.

Best for: commodity building types (warehouses, standard retail fit-outs), projects with hard deadlines, and owners who prioritize schedule certainty over design customization.

CM at Risk
Construction Manager at Risk
Also called: CMAR, CM/GC

The Construction Manager is brought on during design — before documents are complete — to provide pricing input, constructability reviews, and early trade buyout. They carry a Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP) and absorb cost overruns above that figure.

The architect remains your separate design consultant and retains design authority. The CM's early involvement typically reduces surprises in the field. This method adds preconstruction fees but often reduces total project cost through early coordination.

Best for: complex or phased projects, occupied building renovations, healthcare and institutional clients who need schedule certainty alongside design quality.

Scope of Architectural Services

Full-Service Architecture vs. Limited-Scope Engagement

Hiring an architect does not always mean hiring them for everything. Scope can be tailored. Understanding what you are and are not getting prevents the most common owner regrets.

Limited Scope

Design Only / Permit Set Only

Some owners engage an architect for a defined slice: a feasibility study, a concept design, or stamped drawings for permit. Fees are lower, but so is coverage.

  • Drawings sufficient for permit or bidding
  • Lower upfront fee (often flat or hourly)
  • Faster to engage and complete
  • No construction oversight or payment review
  • No RFI responses, shop drawing review, or punch list
  • You absorb field coordination gaps
Full-Service

Programming Through Project Closeout

The architect stays engaged from first conversation through final inspection. Every phase is covered. You have an expert advocate at every decision point.

  • Programming, feasibility, and site analysis
  • Full design phases through permit
  • Bidding and contractor selection support
  • Construction administration and payment certification
  • Punch list, closeout, and warranty support
  • Independent oversight protecting your investment
The fee-saving trap: A limited-scope engagement saves 3–6% in architect fees. But without construction administration, owners commonly pay 5–15% more in change orders that a reviewing architect would have caught. The math rarely favors skipping CA on projects over $500K.

Decision Framework

When Does Each Method Make Sense?

There is no universally correct answer. The right method depends on what you value most and what you can manage. These three scenarios map owner priorities to delivery structures.

Choose Design-Bid-Build if...

You care deeply about design quality, want competitive pricing on construction, have adequate schedule flexibility, and want an architect who answers to you — not to the contractor — throughout the entire project.

This is the right structure for custom homes, civic buildings, institutional campuses, and any project where the design itself is a primary deliverable.

Recommended for most owner-driven projects

Choose Design-Build if...

Speed is your top priority, design is standardized or precedent-driven, and you are comfortable ceding some design decision authority to the delivery team. Works well for tenant build-outs, warehouses, and repetitive facility types.

Have your own attorney review the contract. Ensure performance specifications, not just schematic drawings, define what you are buying.

Proceed with independent owner representation

Choose CM at Risk if...

Your project is complex, phased, or occupied during construction. You want design quality and architect involvement but also need a Guaranteed Maximum Price before construction starts and a construction expert at the table during design.

Budget for preconstruction services — typically 0.5–1.5% of construction cost — as a separate line item in your project budget.

Strong fit for healthcare, higher education, renovation
