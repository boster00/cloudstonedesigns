Your Preparation Checklist

Five things to think through before you arrive.

You don't need all the answers — in fact, some of the best design conversations start from uncertainty. But having considered these five areas will make your consultation far more productive.

01

Site Details

  • Property address and legal description
  • Lot size and approximate dimensions
  • Zoning district (if known)
  • Notable site features: slope, trees, views, access
  • Known easements, setbacks, or restrictions
02

Program & Wish List

  • Number and types of spaces needed
  • Approximate square footage or area goals
  • Special functions (studio, garage, ADU, etc.)
  • Lifestyle priorities and must-haves
  • Aesthetic references (images, words, precedents)
03

Budget Range

  • Overall construction budget (high — low range is fine)
  • Sense of financing vs. cash purchase
  • Priorities if scope needs trimming
  • Contingency comfort level
04

Timeline & Drivers

  • Desired move-in or occupancy date
  • Hard deadlines (lease end, life events)
  • Flexibility in phases or staging
  • Permitting jurisdiction awareness
05

Existing Conditions

  • Current use of the property or building
  • Known structural or mechanical issues
  • History of prior permits or improvements
  • Utility connections (water, sewer, gas, electric)

Documents to Bring

Helpful documents that give us a head start.

These are not requirements — your consultation will be valuable whether or not you have any of them. But if you do have these documents, bringing them allows us to move quickly from conversation into analysis.

  • Boundary Survey

    An official survey showing your property boundaries, easements, and setbacks. If you purchased recently, your title company may have one on file.

  • Preliminary Title Report

    Reveals encumbrances, easements, and restrictions on the property that may affect what can be built — important context for design.

  • Existing Floor Plans or As-Builts

    Any drawings of the current structure — even rough sketches or builder plans. Renovation projects benefit enormously from having this baseline.

  • Site Photos or Video

    Photos of the site, existing structure, views, neighboring context — even taken on your phone. These are invaluable for a remote or early meeting.

  • Inspiration Images

    Screenshots, magazine clippings, a shared folder — whatever has caught your eye. There's no wrong aesthetic reference, and it speeds up the design alignment process greatly.

Questions to Ask

Good questions to bring to your architect.

There are no silly questions in a first consultation. But these are the ones that tend to open the most productive conversations — and that Cloudstone Designs is ready to answer clearly and honestly.

How does your fee structure work, and what does the design process look like?
At Cloudstone Designs, we offer a transparent fee structure typically based on a percentage of construction cost or a negotiated fixed fee, depending on project type. We will walk you through our full process — from schematic design through construction administration — so you know exactly what to expect at each phase and what you're investing in.
Have you worked on projects similar to mine in scope and setting?
Bring a brief description of your project and ask about comparable work in our portfolio. We will share relevant precedents and be candid about areas where your project may present unfamiliar terrain — and how we approach that as a studio.
What is a realistic timeline from design to occupancy?
Project timelines vary significantly based on scope, permitting jurisdiction, and contractor availability. We will give you an honest, phased breakdown — not just an optimistic guess — with the key milestones and decision points that affect schedule.
What is the biggest risk I should know about for this type of project?
We believe in surfacing risks early, not late. In a first meeting, we can typically identify the top one or two category risks for your project type — whether permitting complexity, soil conditions, budget stretch, or program ambiguity — and discuss how they're typically managed.
How do we communicate and make decisions throughout the project?
We will explain our meeting cadence, deliverable review process, and how we handle approvals and scope changes. We value clear, documented communication and will show you the tools and rhythm we use to keep clients informed and empowered at every stage.
What happens if my budget and my wish list don't align?
This is one of the most important conversations in architecture, and we approach it without judgment. We will help you understand scope-to-budget ratios for your project type and explore phasing, value-engineering strategies, or program adjustments that let you build the right project for now — with a clear path toward future goals.
