The six phases

What happens, and when.

Each card below covers one phase. Look for the duration badge, the deliverables list, and the “Your Role” callout — that last one is the most important for first-time clients.

01

Phase one

Pre-Design & Feasibility

2 – 4 weeks Listening & Learning

This is where we learn everything about you, your site, and your goals before a single line is drawn. We review site conditions, zoning regulations, setbacks, and any deed restrictions. We also explore your budget range and priorities so there are no surprises down the road.

Many clients are surprised how much ground we can cover before design begins. The pre-design investment saves time and money in every phase that follows.

What you receive
  • Feasibility summary memorandum
  • Zoning / regulatory summary
  • Preliminary program (room list & area goals)
  • Rough order-of-magnitude budget range
  • Recommended next steps and schedule
Photo: initial site visit or client meeting
Your role in this phase

Share your wish list, your must-haves, and your budget ceiling honestly. Bring photos of spaces you love. Walk the site with us if possible. The more you share now, the more confidently we design later. Your input here shapes every decision that follows.

02

Phase two

Schematic Design

4 – 8 weeks Big ideas take shape

This is where the design comes alive for the first time. We explore multiple conceptual directions — floor plan arrangements, site orientation, massing, and overall character. Drawings at this stage are intentionally loose so options remain easy to modify.

You will see hand sketches or digital diagrams, not finished construction drawings. That is intentional — we want your gut reaction, not your red pen.

What you receive
  • 2–3 design concept options (floor plans, elevations)
  • Site plan showing building footprint and access
  • Preliminary 3-D massing model or sketch renders
  • Updated program confirming square footage by room
  • Schematic cost estimate (order of magnitude)
Placeholder: concept floor plan sketch
Your role in this phase

Review each option and respond honestly. What excites you? What feels off? You do not need technical vocabulary — “this room feels too closed off” is exactly the kind of feedback we need. At the end of this phase, we select one direction to develop. This approval is a key milestone.

03

Phase three

Design Development

6 – 10 weeks Details & Systems

With a single concept approved, we now refine every element. Wall thicknesses become real. Structural systems are coordinated. Mechanical, electrical, and plumbing engineers join the team. You begin making material and finish selections in earnest.

Design development is where the project graduates from “idea” to “buildable thing.” Major changes at this stage become expensive — which is why the earlier phases exist to lock in direction first.

What you receive
  • Fully dimensioned floor plans and elevations
  • Exterior and interior material board / finish schedule
  • Structural, mechanical, and electrical coordination drawings
  • Window and door schedule
  • Design development cost estimate (more refined)
Placeholder: material board or detail drawing
Your role in this phase

Select finishes, fixtures, and materials by the deadlines we provide. Delayed selections cause downstream schedule impacts. We will guide you through decisions with curated options — you will not be handed an endless catalog and told to choose. Respond promptly to coordination questions as they arise.

04

Phase four

Construction Documents

8 – 14 weeks The full instruction set

Construction documents (CDs) are the complete, legally binding set of drawings and specifications that a contractor uses to build your project. They include every dimension, material, assembly detail, and code compliance note a builder needs.

This is the most technically intensive phase and the one least visible to clients — but it determines the quality and accuracy of bids and ultimately of construction itself. Good CDs mean fewer change orders on site.

What you receive
  • Full architectural drawing set (stamped by licensed architect)
  • Structural, MEP, and civil engineering drawings
  • Technical specifications (Division 01–49 as applicable)
  • Bidding documents and instructions to bidders
  • Pre-bid contractor shortlist review (if requested)
Placeholder: drawing set or spec cover sheet
Your role in this phase

Your primary job is to confirm any outstanding selections and respond quickly to clarification questions. Once CDs are complete, you will review bids from contractors we help you identify. We will help you read those bids and select a builder. Avoid scope changes at this stage — each one requires re-drawing and re-checking coordinated sets.

05

Phase five

Permitting

4 – 16 weeks Government review

We submit your construction documents to the local building authority for plan review. The timeline here is largely outside our control — it depends on the jurisdiction, the review queue, and whether the plan checker has comments. We manage all correspondence and respond to any corrections (plan check comments) on your behalf.

This phase often runs in parallel with the final phases of CD production and contractor bidding, so calendar time is minimized.

What you receive
  • Permit application and all required submissions
  • Plan check comment responses (written and drawing revisions)
  • Approved permitted drawing set (the “job copy”)
  • Building permit in your name, ready for construction
Placeholder: permit approval or plan check visual
Your role in this phase

Permitting is largely a waiting game managed by our team. You may need to sign the permit application as the property owner — we will send you exactly what to sign and when. Use this period to finalize your contractor selection and prepare financially for construction mobilization. We will keep you updated at each review milestone.

06

Phase six

Construction Administration

Duration of construction We stay on your side

Construction administration (CA) is often misunderstood. We do not manage the contractor — that is the general contractor’s role. Our job is to be your independent eyes and ears: reviewing submittals, answering contractor RFIs, visiting the site at key milestones, and ensuring what gets built matches what was designed.

We also review contractor payment applications and advise you on whether to approve them. This protects you from paying for work that has not been completed or done correctly.

What you receive
  • Site observation reports (after each architect visit)
  • Submittal & shop drawing review log
  • RFI (Request for Information) log and responses
  • Architect’s supplemental instructions (ASIs) as needed
  • Substantial completion walkthrough and punch list
  • Certificate of Occupancy coordination
  • Project closeout record set (as-builts)
Photo: architect site observation visit
Your role in this phase

Stay accessible. Contractor questions and substitution requests often require timely owner decisions. We will translate every technical question into plain language before it reaches you. At substantial completion, we walk the project together, identify anything not meeting the drawings, and track the contractor’s completion of the punch list. You do not get the keys — or pay the final retainer — until the list is clear.

Common questions

What first-time clients
always ask us.

How much does an architect cost, and when do I pay?
Architectural fees for residential projects typically range from 8% to 15% of construction cost, depending on project complexity and the level of service you retain us for. We can also work on a fixed fee, which many clients prefer for budgeting certainty. Fees are typically billed monthly based on percent-complete within each phase — not lump sums at the end. We will present a detailed fee proposal before you sign anything.
Can I skip phases to save money?
In theory, yes — but in practice, skipping phases almost always costs more than it saves. Jumping straight to construction documents without a schematic design phase means expensive revisions when the layout does not work the way you hoped. Each phase exists to surface and resolve problems at the cheapest possible moment. We are happy to discuss a scope that fits your situation, but we will be honest about the risks.
Do you recommend contractors, or do we find our own?
We can provide a shortlist of contractors we have worked with and trust. You are never required to use them — the final selection is always yours. We recommend getting at least three competitive bids and will help you evaluate each one on an apples-to-apples basis. A good contractor relationship is one of the most important factors in project success, and we take that recommendation seriously.
What if I change my mind mid-process?
Changes early in the process (pre-design, schematic) are easy and inexpensive. Changes during design development require more rework. Changes after construction documents are issued carry significant cost implications. Changes during construction are the most expensive of all. This is not meant to alarm you — it is meant to encourage you to be thorough and honest in the early phases so that your design truly reflects what you want before we commit it to a buildable document set.
How involved do I need to be during construction?
You do not need to visit the site daily — that is the general contractor’s responsibility. We handle technical oversight. What we do ask is that you remain responsive: contractor substitution requests, RFI responses, and payment application reviews often have 48–72 hour windows. We will always translate the technical into plain language before it reaches you. Most clients find site visits exciting rather than burdensome once the walls are up.
What is a Certificate of Occupancy?
A Certificate of Occupancy (CO) is the document issued by your local building authority that confirms the completed building complies with all applicable codes and is legally safe to occupy. It is the official finish line of your project. Without it, you cannot legally move in, and most lenders require it to release final financing. We coordinate all inspections needed to reach CO and will be at the final walkthrough with the building inspector.
