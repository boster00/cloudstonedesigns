01 — Overview

What Each Model Actually Is

The labels vary by market, but the structural differences between these three paths are consistent and significant. Here is what each one covers — and what it does not.

Option A

Full-Service Architect

A licensed architect you hire independently. They work exclusively in your interest across every phase: programming, schematic design, design development, construction documents, permit coordination, contractor bidding, and construction administration.

Phases 1–5 Owner’s advocate Contractor independent
Option B

Design-Build Firm

A single entity that handles both design and construction under one contract. Faster to break ground and often easier to manage, but the designer ultimately answers to the contractor — not to you. Good for streamlined delivery; harder to verify design quality independently.

Integrated delivery Single contract Less owner oversight
Option C

Permit Drawings Only

An architect or draftsperson provides stamped construction documents sufficient for permit approval and basic bidding. No design development, no site visits, no construction oversight. You manage the rest — or hire separately for each piece.

Documents only Owner-managed Lower upfront fee

"Design-build is not inherently inferior. For a straightforward volume build or a time-constrained developer project, it is frequently the right answer. The question is whether the complexity and stakes of your specific project warrant independent design oversight."

— Cloudstone Designs

03 — Tradeoffs

The Real Tradeoffs

Beyond the table. What the decision actually costs you in practice — in money, time, quality, and control.

Cost

The cheapest fee upfront is rarely the cheapest total cost.

Permit-only drawings carry the lowest architectural fee, but that cost shifts to the owner in the form of time, contractor management, and expensive field-level design decisions. Design-build bundles design cost into the construction contract, where margins are harder to interrogate. A full-service fee of 8–15% of construction cost includes every phase of work; the question is whether those services would be purchased separately anyway — and at what premium.

Full-Service: predictable fee, competitive bids Design-Build: bundled margin, hard to compare Permit-Only: low fee, high owner effort
Control

Control over design is inseparable from who the designer answers to.

In a design-build model, the architect or designer is an employee or subcontractor of the builder. When design decisions conflict with cost efficiency, the contractor’s interest typically wins. In a full-service engagement, the architect has a direct obligation to you — they review substitutions, flag scope creep, and push back on contractor value-engineering that compromises your intent. With permit drawings only, there is no advocate in the field at all.

Full-Service: architect as owner’s agent Design-Build: contractor mediates all decisions Permit-Only: owner manages directly
Quality

Quality gaps rarely appear in design; they appear during construction.

The schematic drawings presented in a design-build proposal can look excellent. Quality differences emerge in material substitutions, detailing decisions, and how RFIs (requests for information) are resolved under budget pressure. An architect conducting construction administration catches these in real time and has the contractual authority to reject non-conforming work. Without that independent review, substitutions often go undocumented.

Worth knowing: Construction administration (CA) is frequently one of the first services owners cut to reduce fees. It is also the service most likely to recover its cost through contractor accountability, value-engineering pushback, and prevention of field errors.
Timeline

Faster to contract is not the same as faster to completion.

Design-build can move to a signed GMP (guaranteed maximum price) contract faster, which matters for time-driven projects. But compressed design timelines often resurface as change orders, which extend construction and erode savings. Full-service architecture front-loads time in design and document coordination, which typically results in fewer surprises during the build. Permit-only engagements are fastest on paper but carry the highest risk of field-level delays when undocumented conditions arise.

Design-Build: fast to contract, watch for COs Full-Service: slower to break ground, fewer surprises Permit-Only: fastest documents, highest field risk
Decision Ownership

The most consequential decisions happen in the field, not the design meeting.

Every project generates hundreds of small decisions during construction — a window rough-opening that misses by two inches, a structural beam that needs re-routing, a specified tile that is discontinued. In a full-service engagement, the architect of record makes those calls with full knowledge of your design intent. In design-build, the contractor makes them with full knowledge of their margin. In a permit-only arrangement, you receive a phone call from a framer expecting an answer within the hour.

04 — Right Fit

When Each Model Is the Right Choice

Honest signal matching. If your project fits the design-build column, it should be design-build.

Full-Service Architecture

Custom, high-investment, or technically complex projects

Where the design outcome significantly affects resale value, livability, or regulatory compliance and the cost of an error exceeds the cost of oversight.

  • New construction on a challenging site (slope, setbacks, views)
  • Historic designation, HOA design review, or complex entitlements
  • Projects where the owner is not local or construction-experienced
  • Budgets above $800K where contractor accountability matters
  • Significant additions touching existing structure or systems
  • Clients with a strong design vision and limited time to manage it
Design-Build

Streamlined delivery on a defined program and timeline

Where speed to construction, a predictable lump-sum price, and a simpler coordination structure outweigh the value of independent design oversight.

  • Developer projects with a repeatable unit type or floor plan
  • ADU or small addition with a well-established scope
  • Commercial fit-outs with a proven tenant improvement process
  • Owners who prefer a single point of contact and accountability
  • Time-constrained projects where schedule risk outweighs design risk
Permit Drawings Only

Simple, code-compliant work with an owner who can manage execution

Where the design is essentially settled, the scope is well-defined, and the owner has the time, expertise, and network to manage construction directly.

  • Prefab or kit-home installation requiring a stamp
  • Simple deck, fence, or accessory structure permitted by code table
  • Developer with in-house construction management capacity
  • Repeat build using proven plans with minor site adaptations
  • Owner-builder projects with direct trades relationships

05 — Due Diligence

Questions to Ask Before You Decide

Use these in every interview. The answers matter less than how quickly and specifically each firm provides them.

01

Who will be the licensed architect of record, and will I meet them before signing?

02

Is construction administration included? If not, what is the cost to add it?

03

How is the designer compensated if design-build? Are they employed by the contractor?

04

Can I see examples of your construction documents — not renderings?

05

How are material substitutions during construction handled and approved?

06

What is your typical change order rate as a percentage of original contract?

07

Will I have the ability to competitively bid construction, or is the contractor fixed?

08

Who is responsible for errors or omissions in the construction documents?

09

How many site visits are included during construction, and who conducts them?

10

Can I speak to a former client whose project was similar in scale and complexity to mine?

06 — Warning Signs

Red Flags to Watch For

In any engagement model. These are signals worth pausing on regardless of how well the design presentation went.

  • No written scope of services in the contract Vague scope language is the primary cause of fee disputes and service gaps. Every item in this guide — CA, permitting, contractor coordination — should appear explicitly.
  • A design-build firm that is reluctant to show you a cost breakdown If the design fee cannot be separated from the construction fee, you cannot evaluate whether you are paying a market rate for either. Some firms have legitimate reasons for this; many do not.
  • Construction administration described as “optional” on complex projects On straightforward projects, it may genuinely be optional. On custom new construction or technically complex renovations, this framing suggests the firm may not have the capacity or interest to provide it.
  • Pressure to sign before your questions are fully answered Quality firms expect due diligence and do not pressure timelines. Urgency is occasionally real; it is more often a sales tactic.
  • A fee that is significantly below market with no clear explanation Below-market fees are recovered through scope reduction, change orders, or reduced time on your project. Ask what is not included before comparing numbers.
