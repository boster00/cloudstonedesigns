Before you begin your project

8 Mistakes That Derail Building Projects Before They Start

Most construction disasters are not contractor failures. They are the predictable result of decisions made — or skipped — in the weeks and months before a single permit is filed. Here is what experienced clients wish they had known.

The problems almost always start before a shovel touches the ground.

After working through hundreds of projects, the patterns are unmistakable. Clients who run into serious cost overruns, delays, or legal complications rarely made one catastrophic decision. They made a series of small, understandable missteps — each one reasonable in isolation, each one compounding the next.

This guide covers the eight mistakes we see most often and most expensively. If you are still in early planning, consider this your pre-flight checklist. If you have already started and recognize yourself in one of these sections, the earlier you course-correct, the better.

None of this is meant to alarm you. Building projects can and do go smoothly. But they go smoothly because someone made thoughtful decisions early on — not because they got lucky.

01
Most Costly

Hiring a Contractor Before Design Is Complete

This is the single most expensive mistake on this list, and it is also the most common. A client gets excited, gets a bid from a contractor they trust, and signs a contract before any drawings exist. The contractor starts ordering materials. Then the architect produces drawings that require layout changes. The contractor has to revise scope. Change orders accumulate. The project ends up costing 20–40% more than the original bid.

Contractors bid on what they can see. If design is incomplete, the bid is incomplete. Any contractor who gives you a firm number before permit-ready drawings is either guessing or has built in enough contingency to cover what they do not yet know — and you will pay for that contingency whether you need it or not.

The fix: Do not approach contractors for binding bids until construction documents are finalized and permitted. Schematic-phase estimates are useful for budget calibration — not for signing contracts.
02
Budget Risk

Underbudgeting for Professional Fees

Most clients allocate budget for construction and leave an afterthought for everything else. Architectural fees, engineering consultants, permit fees, inspections, interior design, landscape architecture, and project management can collectively represent 15–25% of total project cost on a custom residential build — sometimes more.

When the fee budget runs short mid-project, one of two things happens: you switch architects (extremely disruptive and often more expensive), or you start cutting scope in the design process — eliminating exactly the oversight that prevents construction errors.

The fix: When calculating your budget, start with total project cost and work backwards. Allocate 15–22% for professional services before you allocate anything to finishes or square footage.

03
Legal Risk

Not Checking Zoning and Code Before You Fall in Love with a Design

Zoning regulations determine what you can build, where you can build it, how tall it can be, how close to property lines, and sometimes what materials you can use. They are public record and they are not negotiable — at least not without a formal variance process that can add six to eighteen months and significant legal fees.

The worst version of this mistake: a client has spent months developing a design, has a full set of drawings, and submits for permit — only to discover that the proposed structure exceeds the allowed floor-area ratio, or that the property is in a coastal zone with additional overlay restrictions, or that the planned addition crosses a setback line by four feet. Starting over is not hypothetical. It happens.

The fix: Zoning research is one of the first things your architect should do — in week one. If you are researching on your own before hiring, call your local planning department. The conversation is free and the information is invaluable.
04
Process Risk

Skipping Schematic Design to "Save Time"

Schematic design is where options are explored, tested, and eliminated. It is where an architect looks at three different approaches to a floor plan and helps you understand the trade-offs before committing to one. It is not a formality.

Clients who skip or rush schematic design to get to construction documents faster inevitably discover, partway through construction documents — or worse, during construction itself — that the plan has a fundamental problem: rooms are the wrong size, adjacencies do not work, circulation is awkward, or the structure is fighting the architecture. Solving a floor plan problem in schematic design takes hours. Solving it in construction documents takes weeks. Solving it during construction is measured in tens of thousands of dollars.

The fix: Treat schematic design as the most valuable phase of the project. Ask your architect to develop at least two genuinely different options before settling on a direction. The time spent here is the best investment on the project.
05
Process Risk

Rushing Into Construction Documents Without a Resolved Design

Construction documents (CDs) are the set of drawings and specifications that contractors build from and municipalities review for permits. They need to be precise, coordinated, and complete. Producing CDs on top of an unresolved design is like writing a final draft on top of a first-draft outline — the problems stack.

When design decisions are still open when CD production begins, the documents get produced in parallel with ongoing decision-making. Sheets get revised, consultants get re-issued, and coordination errors accumulate. The permit set ends up inconsistent. Contractors encounter contradictions in the field and write change orders. The original schedule assumptions collapse.

The fix: Establish a formal design freeze before CDs begin. Every open design question — materials, dimensions, structural system, mechanical approach — should be resolved and owner-approved before the first CD is drafted.
06
Selection Risk

Choosing an Architect Based on Fee Alone

Architecture fees vary for the same reason contractor bids vary: experience, capacity, process rigor, and the depth of documentation all differ. A lower fee can mean faster timelines, lighter documentation, or less senior involvement in your project. Any of those outcomes can be fine depending on what you are building. What matters is that you understand what you are buying.

A thin set of construction documents costs less to produce and generates more change orders and field questions during construction. The savings on the design fee are typically consumed several times over in contractor time, delays, and unresolved field issues. The more complex the project, the more this math compounds.

The fix: When interviewing architects, ask specifically about their documentation standards and how they handle field administration. Ask to see a sample specification. Ask how many projects a project manager carries at one time. The answers tell you more than the fee schedule.
07
Budget Risk

Treating the Budget as a Rough Number Until Bids Come In

"We will figure out the real budget once we see bids" is a phrase that precedes a very specific kind of project failure. Design decisions made without a firm budget are often incompatible with any realistic bid. When bids come in over budget, value engineering begins — which means redesigning work already done, repricing materials, renegotiating scope, and reissuing drawings. This process takes months and costs real money in architectural fees.

A well-established budget, set in the first two weeks of the project and stress-tested with a cost estimator during schematic design, is not a constraint. It is a tool. It tells your architect what design solutions are worth developing and which ones to set aside.

The fix: Engage a construction cost estimator during schematic design — not at bid time. A schematic-phase estimate from an independent estimator can be had for a few thousand dollars and can prevent redesign costs ten times larger.
08
Timing Risk

Waiting Too Long to Bring an Architect In

Some clients begin by working with a design-build contractor, a draftsperson, or an online floor-plan service before engaging a licensed architect. By the time they recognize what is missing — code compliance, structural coordination, permit-readiness, site analysis — they have already committed to a layout or a structural approach that an architect would not have recommended.

Early architectural involvement is not a luxury. On any project requiring permits, structural work, or significant site modification, an architect's input in the first two weeks influences decisions that will be locked in for the life of the building. Waiting until late in pre-design — or until after a contractor is onboard — forfeits that influence.

The fix: If you are still in the early thinking stage — sketching ideas, looking at lots, considering scope — that is exactly the right time to have a first conversation with an architect. Most firms offer a no-commitment initial consultation.

Questions clients often ask at this stage

  • It depends on the jurisdiction and the scope. Many municipalities require a licensed architect's stamp for any structural work, any change of occupancy, or any project above a certain square footage threshold — even additions. Beyond the legal requirement, an architect on even a modest addition coordinates structural, mechanical, and envelope decisions in ways that significantly reduce field problems. The question is not whether you need one; it is whether the project is simple enough that basic drafting services would suffice. Most additions are not that simple.
  • The best first check is a published cost-per-square-foot range for your region, building type, and quality level. The RSMeans database and local contractor associations publish these annually. Apply that range to your intended program, add 15–22% for professional fees, add 8–12% for contingency, and compare to your total available budget. If the numbers do not match, the earlier you know, the better — before you have invested anything in design.
  • A first meeting should establish the project scope, timeline, and budget you have in mind — and give the architect enough information to identify any obvious constraints or risks. A good first meeting also gives you a clear sense of the firm's communication style and how they structure their process. Ask: What does your pre-design phase include? How do you handle changes after design freeze? Who will be my primary contact? Those three questions reveal more than a portfolio review.
  • Design-build consolidates design and construction under one contract, which can streamline communication and accountability. It addresses some of the coordination problems in Mistakes 1 and 5. However, it also removes an important structural check: independent architect oversight during construction. In a traditional delivery, your architect is contractually obligated to act in your interest, not the contractor's. In design-build, that independent review is gone. For straightforward projects with a trusted firm, that trade-off can be worth it. For complex or high-value projects, it deserves careful consideration.
